India in the Persianate Age

Long before the Mughals, Turkic cavalry and Persian culture were already reshaping the subcontinent. Richard M. Eaton's India in the Persianate Age traces seven centuries of encounter — from Mahmud of Ghazni's raids to Aurangzeb's overreach — in four episodes that reveal how Islam and Hinduism never simply collided but interpenetrated, translated, and transformed each other in ways that still shape South Asia today.

Episode 1

Turkic Ascendancy and Sultanate Power

Mahmud of Ghazni sacks the Somnath temple. Across South Asia, two grammars of power are about to collide.

October 1025. Mahmud of Ghazni rides out of Afghanistan with thirty thousand cavalry, crosses the Indus, traverses the Thar Desert, and arrives at the great Somnath temple on Gujarat's coast. He besieges it, plunders it, and orders its sacred image broken and carried back to Ghazni as a trophy of Islamic conquest. The episode opens with this iconic scene — then immediately complicates it. Mahmud's raid is not simply a religious war against Hindus: it is one form of imperial display among several. The Chola kings of South India also sack temples — other kings' temples — as acts of political dominance. Two grammars of power share the same landscape. Over the next three centuries, the Delhi Sultanate transforms from vulnerable frontier foothold into durable urban power. Delhi becomes a major Islamic city drawing soldiers, scholars, and poets from Central Asia. Hindu kingdoms adapt. The subcontinent emerges not as a simple Hindu or Muslim space but as something far more complex — and far more interesting.

Episode 2

After Timur: Deccan Courts and the South

Timur sacks Delhi in 1398. Imperial authority fragments. Regional courts flourish.

1398. Timur — Tamerlane — sacks Delhi and departs, leaving the sultanate shattered. Imperial authority fragments across the subcontinent. Regional courts emerge in Bengal, Kashmir, Gujarat, Malwa, and the Deccan. The Bahmani kingdom becomes a center of elegant government, patronage, and court culture before splitting into five rival successor states. In the far south, Vijayanagara rises as a major Hindu empire, using sacred kingship and temple cities to reshape southern political life. The episode traces a profound cultural transformation: Persian language, Persian court protocol, and Persianate aesthetic — miniature painting, garden design, poetry — spread not only through Muslim courts but into Hindu ones. When Marlowe's play Tamburlaine premiered in London in 1587, English audiences saw a terrifying image of Asian conquest. In India, the reality was more complex: regional courts were developing sophisticated administrative systems while Sanskrit and Persian scholarship flourished side by side, in the same libraries, under the same patronage.

Episode 3

Mughal Consolidation and High Empire

Babur defeats Ibrahim Lodi at Panipat. A new empire begins — and a new way of thinking about India.

April 1526. Babur, a Timurid prince from Fergana who has already lost and retaken Samarkand twice, defeats the Sultan of Delhi at Panipat using superior artillery and disciplined cavalry. He founds the Mughal Empire almost as an afterthought — he wanted Samarkand, not India. His son Humayun loses the empire to the Afghans, spends fifteen years in exile, and fights his way back with Persian support. Akbar, inheriting as a boy of thirteen, becomes the great consolidator: he develops sophisticated administrative systems, incorporates Hindu nobility into the highest offices, and articulates a policy of religious tolerance that makes his court one of the most cosmopolitan institutions of the sixteenth century. The episode traces the Mughal system at its height — Shah Jahan's Taj Mahal, Jahangir's court paintings, the fusion of Sanskritic and Persianate culture that produces something entirely new. Then comes the succession crisis that Akbar's own system could not prevent: four sons, one throne, an empire that will pass to whoever survives.

Episode 4

Aurangzeb, Imperial Strain, and Transition

Aurangzeb buries his brothers, conquers the Deccan, and exhausts an empire. The Mughals will never recover.

Aurangzeb eliminates his brothers in a succession struggle of extraordinary brutality — most notably Dara Shikoh, his eldest brother and intellectual rival, whose vision of Islam-Hindu synthesis he regards as heresy. He rules for nearly fifty years, the longest Mughal reign, during which he pushes imperial territory to its greatest extent — conquering the Deccan sultanates, extending control deep into South India. But the campaigns drain the treasury and keep the emperor permanently in the field, far from his capital, managing a vast empire from a tent. Regional governors grow more independent. Maratha confederations resist. Sikh communities organize militarily against his religious policies. Beneath the spectacle of imperial power, something else is underway: commerce and cash wealth are shifting away from land-based taxation, regional identities are consolidating, and the British East India Company — still a minor trading concern — is quietly building the military capacity that will transform it into something unrecognizable. When Aurangzeb dies in 1707, the decline has already begun.
