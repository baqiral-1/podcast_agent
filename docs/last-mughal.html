The Last Mughal

In 1857, the last Mughal emperor was eighty-two years old, a poet-king with no real power and a court in elegant decline. Then the sepoys arrived, and everything changed. William Dalrymple's The Last Mughal follows Bahadur Shah Zafar from the rebellion that made him briefly real again, through the siege that destroyed his city, to the exile that erased him — and asks what was lost when Delhi fell.

Episode 1

Delhi Before the Deluge

The last Mughal emperor writes poetry while the world outside his palace window is about to ignite.

Bahadur Shah Zafar II is eighty-two years old. He is a calligrapher, a poet, a Sufi scholar, and a gardener of some distinction. He is not, in any practical sense, a ruler: his authority extends no further than the Red Fort's walls, and even there it is constrained by the British political resident who controls his stipend. Delhi in 1856 is a city of extraordinary cultural refinement — one of the most cosmopolitan places on earth — but also a city in which British evangelical missionaries are working to replace the old world of Hindu-Muslim coexistence with a new Christian order. Zafar watches, and writes. In the bazaars, rumors circulate about rifle cartridges greased with pig and cow fat. In Meerut, sepoys refuse to bite them. On May 10, 1857, they shoot their officers, free their imprisoned comrades, and ride through the night toward Delhi — toward the only living symbol of Mughal authority — seeking a legitimacy that only an old, cautious emperor can give.

Episode 2

The Explosion of May

The sepoys crowd into the Hall of Audience. They ask Zafar to place his hands on their heads. He hesitates. Then he does.

May 11, 1857. The Hall of Audience in the Red Fort. Sepoys who arrived from Meerut overnight — grimy, exhausted, still carrying weapons — crowd forward and ask the emperor to place his hand on their heads: a blessing that transforms a mutiny into an imperial revolution. Zafar hesitates. His caution is legendary. Then he chooses. His hand descends. Half a mile away, Lieutenant George Willoughby detonates the great magazine, sending a massive explosion through the city's center that kills hundreds. In a single morning: an imperial blessing and a military catastrophe, synchronized. British survivors flee. The Ridge north of the city becomes a British camp — but instead of falling as expected, Delhi fills with rebel forces from across northern India. The British besieging force finds itself effectively besieged. The Red Fort fills with soldiers who have no unified command. And in the palace at the center of the storm, the old poet-king has placed his hand on something he cannot take back.

Episode 3

Siege, Stalemate, and the Turn of the Tide

For three months, the British hold the Ridge. Delhi holds them back. Then the reinforcements arrive.

Three months. British forces hold the Ridge in conditions of extraordinary suffering: cholera, dysentery, heat, flies, constant sniping, and the knowledge that every day without decisive action means another day of rebel reinforcement. Inside Delhi, the rebellion fragments. Factions within the city dispute strategy, authority, and resources. Zafar manages his court — still holding ceremonies, still writing poetry, still trying to manage fractious military commanders who will not listen to an old man who never wanted this war. On August 1, Eid al-Adha. The city is in the grip of a potential communal explosion. Zafar's careful precautions prevent it. Outside the city, British reinforcements finally arrive. Artillery is positioned. Breaching batteries are established. The balance begins to shift — not dramatically, but with the slow accumulation of weight. By early September, the siege is breaking open. The final assault on Delhi's walls is days away. The city that has survived three months is about to fall in days.

Episode 4

Fall, Retribution, and Exile

Zafar is found in a tomb, brought back to Delhi in a palanquin — no longer a sovereign, not even a prisoner. Something smaller.

September 1857. British forces breach the walls and fight through Delhi in days of brutal street combat. Nicholson, the legendary British officer whose reckless courage defines the assault, is mortally wounded and dies days later. Zafar flees to Humayun's Tomb and is found there, brought back to Delhi in a palanquin — not as sovereign, not as ceremonial prisoner, but as something smaller, something already half-erased. William Hodson, the British cavalry officer who captures him, executes three Mughal princes in cold blood the same day, on a Delhi street, in front of a crowd. Zafar is tried, convicted of rebellion, and exiled to Rangoon in 1858, where he dies in 1862. His grave is made level with the turf so that it will disappear. It does not disappear. From the ruins of 1857, two Muslim futures emerge: one looking westward toward modern education at Aligarh, one looking inward toward Islamic revival at Deoband. The poet-emperor is gone. His shadow remains.
