Black Wave

In 1979, three seismic events shattered the Middle East: a revolution in Iran, a siege in Mecca, and an invasion of Afghanistan. What followed was a forty-year rivalry between two regional powers that weaponized religion, crushed dissent, and exported extremism across the globe. This series traces how that competition devoured intellectuals, empowered fanatics, and tore apart nations from Egypt to Iraq to Pakistan. Through assassinations, proxy wars, and the rise of movements that no one could control, the story reveals how a generation's hopes were systematically destroyed.

Episode 1

Revolution, Siege, and Invasion

1979 begins with a question no one can answer: what happened to us? In Iran, cassette tapes carrying a cleric's sermons fuel a revolution that topples a dynasty. The Shah flees into exile as a new Islamic republic rises from the wreckage. In Mecca, armed zealots storm the holiest site in Islam, triggering a siege that ends in bloodshed and mass executions. The Saudi kingdom, shaken to its core, responds by doubling down on religious conservatism. Then Saddam Hussein plunges into war with Iran, and the entire region begins to burn.

The year 1979 detonates like a bomb across the Middle East, sending shockwaves that will reverberate for decades. In Iran, Ayatollah Khomeini's revolution unfolds with terrifying speed, fueled by smuggled cassette tapes and mass demonstrations that bring millions into the streets. The Shah flees on January 16th, and within weeks a new Islamic republic is declared. Yasser Arafat flies to Tehran seeking alliance with the revolutionary regime, hoping to harness its energy for the Palestinian cause. But the revolution's ambitions extend far beyond Iran's borders. In Mecca, a group of armed zealots led by Juhayman al-Otaybi seizes the Grand Mosque, Islam's holiest site, in a stunning act of insurrection. The siege drags on for two weeks of bloody combat inside the sacred precincts before Saudi forces, assisted by French commandos, finally retake the compound. The Saudi kingdom responds with mass executions and, paradoxically, accelerates its own religious conservatism, pouring billions into fundamentalist education worldwide. Meanwhile, Saddam Hussein watches the chaos in Iran and sees opportunity. He launches a devastating invasion, plunging both nations into a war that will consume a generation. Three events in a single year set in motion a rivalry and a radicalization that no one will be able to reverse.

Episode 2

Assassinations, Jihad, and the Battle for Mecca

A hail of grenades at a presidential reviewing stand. Anwar Sadat crumples as his assassins open fire, and Egypt's experiment with moderation dies with him. In Pakistan, a military dictator imposes harsh Islamic laws that reshape society and fuel sectarian hatred. The Israeli invasion of Beirut draws Iran deeper into Lebanon's chaos. Saudi-funded anti-Shia literature floods the Muslim world, and when Iranian pilgrims and Saudi forces clash in Mecca, the rivalry between two kingdoms reaches a terrifying new intensity. No one is safe.

The aftershocks of 1979 ripple outward with devastating force. In Egypt, President Anwar Sadat's blindness to the rising tide of extremism proves fatal. On October 6, 1981, Lieutenant Khalid Islambouli and his conspirators leap from a military vehicle during a parade and shower the presidential reviewing stand with grenades and gunfire. Sadat falls, and with him dies a vision of Arab moderation. In Pakistan, General Zia ul-Haq launches an ambitious Islamization campaign that transforms the country's legal system, imposes brutal new restrictions on women, and deliberately inflames sectarian hatred against Shia communities. The consequences will poison Pakistani society for decades. Meanwhile, Israel's 1982 invasion of Lebanon unleashes a chain of violence that draws Iran into direct confrontation, as Revolutionary Guard units deploy to the Bekaa Valley and help create Hezbollah. The Saudi-Iranian rivalry intensifies as Saudi-backed anti-Shia propaganda floods mosques and madrasas across the Muslim world. The tension reaches a horrifying climax when Iranian pilgrims and Saudi security forces clash violently during the Hajj in Mecca, leaving hundreds dead and deepening the sectarian fault line that now runs through the heart of Islam. Every attempt at containment only spreads the contagion further.

Episode 3

Radicalization, 9/11, and the Unraveling of Iraq

An Egyptian professor is declared an apostate for his academic writings. A young Saudi plunges into fundamentalism, then dares to speak out after the towers fall. In Baghdad, Saddam's statue topples and the real nightmare begins. Sectarian militias carve up neighborhoods. A shrine bombing in Samarra shatters the last hope for Iraqi unity. The killing of one terrorist leader changes nothing. The fracture spreads like wildfire, and the forces unleashed will soon engulf an entire region in ways no one predicted.

The war on free thought claims its victims one by one. In Egypt, professor Nasr Abu Zeid is targeted for his intellectual work, declared an apostate by an Egyptian court, and forced to flee the country with his wife. His case becomes a chilling symbol of the siege on independent thought that follows the assassination of writer Farag Foda. In Saudi Arabia, a young man named Mansour al-Nogaidan plunges deep into the fundamentalist underground, firebombing a video store before an extraordinary personal transformation leads him to publicly denounce the system that radicalized him. The September 11th attacks accelerate everything. When American forces topple Saddam Hussein's regime in 2003, the aftermath is catastrophic. Baghdad descends into chaos as the state collapses overnight. Sectarian violence erupts between Sunni and Shia communities, with death squads roaming neighborhoods and bodies appearing in rivers each morning. Abu Musab al-Zarqawi's network wages a campaign of spectacular atrocities designed to provoke civil war. The bombing of the sacred Askari shrine in Samarra in February 2006 achieves exactly that, shattering any remaining illusion of Iraqi unity. Even Zarqawi's death in an American airstrike fails to halt the spreading catastrophe. The sectarian fracture now runs too deep to be healed by any single military strike.

Episode 4

Arab Spring, ISIS, and the Fight for the Future

A governor's assassination in Pakistan exposes how deep the extremism has spread. The Arab Spring erupts with electric hope, then curdles into chaos, civil war, and a new kind of terror. ISIS declares a caliphate across the ruins of Iraq and Syria while Saudi Arabia and Iran wage proxy battles across the region. A journalist walks into a consulate and never walks out. The forty-year rivalry reaches its most dangerous chapter yet, and the question that opened this story still has no answer.

The final chapter of a forty-year unraveling begins with a murder that shocks Pakistan. Governor Salmaan Taseer of Punjab is gunned down by his own bodyguard for defending a Christian woman accused of blasphemy. The assassin becomes a folk hero, showered with rose petals by lawyers and clerics, revealing how profoundly extremism has penetrated mainstream society. Then the Arab Spring ignites across North Africa and the Middle East, a cascade of popular uprisings fueled by decades of repression and the electrifying promise of democratic change. But the hopes of millions are systematically crushed. In Syria, peaceful protests are met with barrel bombs, and a civil war spirals into one of the century's worst humanitarian catastrophes. From the wreckage, the Islamic State surges forth, declaring a caliphate that stretches across the shattered borders of Iraq and Syria and attracting fighters from around the globe. Saudi Arabia and Iran pour resources into competing proxy wars across Yemen, Bahrain, Lebanon, and beyond, each seeking regional dominance at any cost. The murder of journalist Jamal Khashoggi inside the Saudi consulate in Istanbul lays bare the ruthlessness of the new generation of leaders. Forty years after the triple earthquake of 1979, the rivalry that reshaped the Middle East shows no sign of resolution.
