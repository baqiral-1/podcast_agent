The Question of Palestine

Eight episodes. Ten books.

The series opens by establishing the conflict’s foundational paradox: how a movement born from persecution became an instrument of dispossession, and how every voice in the corpus — however sympathetic to one side — converges on this structural diagnosis. Episodes 2–3 stage the sharpest disagreements: Was 1948 premeditated or emergent? Was Britain cynical or incompetent? Did violence help or hurt?

Episodes 4–5 reveal the shared foundations beneath these disagreements: the pattern of biased mediation, the demographic obsession that drives all policy, the gap between public rhetoric and private planning.

Episodes 6–7 show how these converging forces produced the occupation system, the Oslo mirage, and the global export of domination technologies. Episode 8 confronts the moral labyrinth that all authors enter but none escape: can two peoples share one land when every proposed solution requires losses both consider existential? The arc moves from apparent disagreement through structural convergence to irreducible moral complexity.

Drawn from 10 works including O Jerusalem!, Righteous Victims, The Hundred Years’ War on Palestine, The Question of Palestine, The Ethnic Cleansing of Palestine, and others.

The series opens by establishing the foundational question: Is the Israel-Palestine conflict best understood as a colonial enterprise, a clash of national movements, or both? Drawing on voices from across the corpus, the episode traces how early Zionist leaders consciously adopted colonial language and methods while simultaneously responding to genuine persecution. It reveals the ‘land without a people’ myth as the foundational erasure that enabled all subsequent dispossession, and introduces the demographic obsession that would drive policy for 125 years. The episode establishes that all authors — even the most sympathetic Israeli voice — converge on the recognition that Zionism operated within a colonial-settler logic, while disagreeing sharply on whether this diagnosis delegitimizes the enterprise.

The Catastrophe

1:33:33

Stages the sharpest historiographical debate in the corpus: Was 1948 a premeditated ethnic cleansing or the cumulative product of wartime chaos? Reveals convergence on outcomes even where causation is contested.

This episode confronts 1948 — the foundational event that created both Israel and the Palestinian refugee crisis. It stages the corpus’s sharpest historiographical debate: Was the displacement of 700,000+ Palestinians a premeditated plan of ethnic cleansing or the cumulative product of wartime chaos? Through the paradigmatic case of Lydda, it shows how military orders, political calculation, and human suffering converge in a single event that crystallizes the Nakba’s meaning. The episode reveals that even where causation is fiercely contested, all accounts converge on the outcome: the transformation of Palestine’s demographic character and Israel’s categorical refusal of refugee return.

The Iron Wall

1:49:37

Traces the pattern of imperial enablement from Balfour through the British Mandate, revealing how every supposed neutral broker was structurally aligned with one side.

This episode reveals the pattern of the structurally biased mediator — the recurring dynamic in which every major power that positions itself as neutral broker is structurally aligned with one side. It traces this pattern from Britain’s contradictory wartime promises through the Mandate’s asymmetric treatment of Jewish and Arab communities, to the United States’ post-1967 role as Israel’s patron disguised as mediator. The episode shows how Jabotinsky’s 1923 ‘iron wall’ doctrine — the idea that colonization could only proceed behind a barrier of force — was successively embodied by the British military, the IDF, and American diplomatic protection.

The Moral Labyrinth

1:47:06

Confronts the irreducible tension at the conflict’s heart: two peoples, one land, no clean exit. Stages the final convergence and the questions that remain open.

The series culminates by confronting the irreducible moral tension at the conflict’s heart: that the survival and flourishing of the Jewish people in Palestine required the dispossession of another people, and that no amount of historical analysis can dissolve this tension. The episode stages the final convergence — Shavit’s agonized Zionism against Said’s structural critique, both acknowledging the same facts but differing on whether Jewish survival needs can morally justify Palestinian dispossession. It traces the epistemic shift from two-state solution to apartheid diagnosis, examines whether Israel can be both Jewish and democratic, and confronts the four paths that remain: ethnic cleansing, apartheid, binational state, or withdrawal. The episode does not resolve but deepens, showing how both peoples are trapped in a moral labyrinth from which no clean exit is visible.
