Return of a King

A narrative history collection tracing the rise of Shah Shuja, the First Afghan War, and the imperial disaster that reshaped Afghanistan and Britain alike.

Episode 1

Exile, Intrigue, and the Road to Intervention

Shah Shuja returns to Kabul under British protection, a restoration that already carries the seeds of occupation.

Shah Shuja’s return to Kabul in 1839 looks like restoration, but it is powered by British bayonets and imperial anxiety about Russia and Persia. We rewind through his earlier reign, the loss of the Koh-i-Noor, exile, and the long intrigue with Sikh and British officials who see Afghanistan as a buffer state. The episode traces how the East India Company chooses to reinstall a pliant monarch, assembling the Army of the Indus and bargaining with Ranjit Singh for passage. Kabul is entered without celebration, and the price of occupation begins to show even before the fighting starts. The story is one of misplaced confidence: an empire convinced it can engineer legitimacy by force, and an Afghan court that returns home tethered to foreign power.

Episode 2

Conquest, Occupation, and Revolt in Kabul

An occupation built on misreading local anger collapses as Kabul turns on its British garrison.

The occupation of Kabul quickly curdles into revolt. British officers misread local anger, levy new taxes, and camp outside the city in an exposed cantonment while Shah Shuja’s authority withers. The episode centers on November 1841, when Alexander Burnes is besieged in his house and killed while commanders hesitate. As mobs surge through Kabul, officers debate half-measures and ignore warnings from allies who urge decisive action. Shah Shuja retreats to the Bala Hisar as Akbar Khan and tribal leaders close the roads, leaving the garrison isolated. We trace the unraveling of the occupation: assassinations, supply failures, and the inability of the garrison to control a city that no longer consents. By the time the British agree to withdraw, they are already bargaining from weakness. The Afghan uprising is not sudden chaos; it is a response to arrogance, interference, and the collapse of an imposed order.

Episode 3

Retreat, Retribution, and the War's Long Shadow

The winter retreat from Kabul becomes legend, followed by a punitive return that cannot erase the defeat.

The retreat from Kabul becomes a catastrophe. British and Indian troops, camp followers, and hostages attempt to march through winter passes toward Jalalabad, only to be cut down in ambush after ambush. Inside Jalalabad, a besieged garrison debates whether to hold out or escape, while captives such as Lady Sale maneuver for survival. Only a handful of survivors reach safety, turning the retreat into imperial legend. The episode follows the disaster’s aftermath: Pollock’s relief force, a punitive march back to Kabul, and the burning of bazaars in retribution. The punitive expedition rescues prisoners but cannot erase the humiliation. The war ends not with victory but with exhaustion and a chastened empire that restores the old Afghan order it tried to displace. The long shadow is a warning about occupation, arrogance, and the limits of imperial rescue narratives.
