Return of a King

A narrative history collection tracing the rise of Shah Shuja, the First Afghan War, and the imperial disaster that reshaped Afghanistan and Britain alike.

Episode 1

Shah Shuja, Empire, and the Road to Invasion

This episode follows the long, unstable rise of Shah Shuja, from dynastic intrigue and exile to the widening imperial contest that drew Afghanistan into the Great Game.

We trace the recovery of the Koh-i-Nur, the fracture of the Durrani world, British anxiety over Napoleon, Russia, and Persia, and the strategic calculations that turned Afghan succession into an imperial project. As diplomats, spies, and rival courts maneuver across Kabul, Lahore, and British India, the story builds toward invasion. What begins as a struggle over legitimacy and alliance becomes the opening act of a much larger war, shaped by ambition, misreading, and the dangerous confidence of empire.

Episode 2

Occupation, Revolt, and the Catastrophe in Kabul

This episode follows the First Afghan War as invasion hardens into occupation and occupation collapses into revolt.

Shah Shuja returns, British armies seize key strongholds, and the politics of Kabul grow steadily more brittle as imperial confidence outruns local reality. We move through court intrigue, Russian and British rivalry, military misjudgment, and the daily humiliations that turn discontent into open resistance. From the uprising in Kabul to the murder of Macnaghten and the disastrous retreat through the passes, the war becomes a study in overreach, indecision, and catastrophic command. By the end, the occupying force that entered Afghanistan in triumph is broken, and the logic of the campaign lies in ruins.

Episode 3

Retribution, Return, and the War's Long Shadow

This episode traces the war's violent finale and its enduring aftermath.

We begin with Jalalabad, where British forces recover their nerve, then follow Pollock and Nott through the punitive march back to Kabul, the release of prisoners, the destruction of cities and bazaars, and the retaliatory massacres that expose the moral cost of imperial revenge. From there the story turns outward: Shah Shuja is gone, Dost Mohammad returns, British victory parades ring hollow, and the conflict leaves behind financial waste, political humiliation, and a country no easier to control than before. The episode closes on the war's long echo, from Crimea to modern Afghanistan, where the same illusions return.
