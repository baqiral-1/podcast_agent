Return of a King

In 1839, the British Empire invaded Afghanistan to install a king nobody wanted. What followed was one of history's most catastrophic military disasters: an occupation, an uprising, a retreat through mountain passes that turned into a massacre. William Dalrymple reconstructs the First Anglo-Afghan War in six episodes, drawing on Persian, Afghan, and British sources — and finding, in a nineteenth-century catastrophe, a mirror for every foreign intervention that has come since.

Episode 1

Exile, Empire, and the Afghan Succession Crisis

Shah Shuja sits in Ludhiana, humiliated and powerless. A British diplomat arrives asking uncomfortable questions.

Shah Shuja ul-Mulk was once the King of Afghanistan. Now he lives in Ludhiana on a British pension, his treasure confiscated, his ambitions tolerated as long as they stay small. When Mountstuart Elphinstone visits Peshawar in 1808 on a British diplomatic mission, he encounters the polished splendor of an Afghan court — fountains, gilt domes, armored cavalry — and a ruler who still commands genuine loyalty. Dost Mohammad Khan, who has since taken Kabul, is something different: a pragmatic leader with broad Afghan support and no particular love for British plans. As Russian expansion pushes south through Central Asia, British officials in Calcutta grow anxious. They want a friendly Afghanistan on their northwestern frontier. Alexander Burnes arrives in Kabul to assess whether Dost Mohammad can be turned into a British ally. He finds a ruler who wants Peshawar returned from the Sikhs. Calcutta refuses. The episode closes with a question no one in London has yet thought to ask: what happens if the Afghans say no?

Episode 2

The Great Game Begins

A Russian agent arrives in Kabul. The British decide they must act — and reach for a king nobody wants.

A Russian envoy, Jan Vitkevitch, arrives in Kabul offering Dost Mohammad an alliance that Britain refuses to provide. The news reaches Calcutta and triggers a decision that Lord Auckland will spend years regretting: if Dost Mohammad leans toward Russia, Britain will remove him and restore Shah Shuja — the exiled king on his pension in Ludhiana — at bayonet point. Herat is under siege by Persian armies. The strategic clock is ticking. Burnes, who has told Calcutta that Dost Mohammad commands genuine Afghan loyalty and that the Russian threat is overblown, is overruled. His warnings are filed. The plan proceeds. The Army of the Indus is assembled — tens of thousands of British and East India Company troops, with sixty thousand camp followers, hundreds of camels, and enough supplies for a short, victorious campaign. Nobody in Calcutta asks what happens after the campaign is over, or how Shah Shuja will govern a country that did not choose him.

Episode 3

The Mouth of Hell

The army descends into the Bolan Pass. The camels begin to die. The rations disappear.

The Army of the Indus has crossed the Indus and entered the Bolan Pass. On paper, the campaign looks orderly: two prongs of invasion, converging on Kandahar and then Kabul, with Shah Shuja restored to his throne in time for the monsoon season. In practice, the Bolan Pass is a catastrophe. Rations fall from half to quarter. Camp followers claw at survival on fried sheep skins, clotted blood, and roots. Horses too weak to continue are shot; baggage burns where it falls; the air reeks of carrion. Sita Ram, a sepoy who will survive to write his memoirs, calls it 'the mouth of hell.' The episode traces how the invasion's greatest dangers come not from Afghan resistance but from British overconfidence — a certainty that superiority in weapons and organization is equivalent to superiority in understanding. By the time the army emerges from the Bolan, it is already weaker than it knows, and the real test has not yet begun.

Episode 4

Occupation of Kabul and the Road to Revolt

The British take Kabul. Shah Shuja is restored. Nobody in the city believes it will last.

The Army of the Indus takes Kandahar, storms Ghazni by night, and enters Kabul in triumph in August 1839. Shah Shuja is restored. Dost Mohammad flees into the mountains. The British build a cantonment outside Kabul, import their food and their customs, and spend two years convincing themselves that the occupation is going well. It is not. Shah Shuja commands no loyalty — he is seen as a British puppet, maintained in power by foreign bayonets. The British cut the subsidies to tribal chiefs who had agreed to keep mountain passes clear. The chiefs stop keeping the passes clear. Alexander Burnes, now the political officer in Kabul, fills his reports with optimism and his diary with dread. In November 1841, crowds gather outside his house. Then the crowd becomes something else. On November 2, Burnes is killed in the street. Senior officers in the cantonment receive the news and do nothing. The retreat that follows will become one of the most catastrophic in British military history.

Episode 5

The Retreat and Disaster in the Passes

The retreat begins in January. In knee-deep snow. With 16,000 people. Only one survives.

January 6, 1842. The British Army of Kabul begins its retreat in knee-high snow, temperatures well below zero, 3,800 sepoys and 700 European troops and approximately 12,000 camp followers descending into the passes. Shah Shuja urges them not to go. He knows what will happen. In the Khord Kabul Pass, Afghan forces fire from the heights above. Men freeze where they fall. Discipline collapses in hours. Lady Sale, wounded in the arm, keeps walking and keeps a diary. Her son-in-law Lieutenant Sturt is stabbed and carried forward until he dies. By the second night, exhausted men lie in the snow while Afghan horsemen strip and kill the camp followers. Of the 16,500 who begin the retreat, one man reaches Jalalabad. Dr. William Brydon, half-dead on a nearly dead horse, rides in on January 13. Behind him: nothing. The most complete military disaster in British imperial history, over in less than a week.

Episode 6

Retribution, Restoration, and Historical Verdict

Britain sends the Army of Retribution. It burns villages, destroys crops, and achieves nothing lasting.

Britain cannot absorb the humiliation without response. The Army of Retribution arrives — Pollock from the east, Nott from the south — and defeats Afghan forces in the field. Jalalabad had held throughout the winter; Sale's garrison attacks rather than endures. Kabul is retaken. The cantonment is burned. The great covered bazaar of Kabul — one of the finest in Central Asia — is destroyed as punishment for the massacre. Villages are razed, crops burned, orchards cut down. Then the army withdraws. It takes the hostages. It leaves behind the same political situation that existed before the invasion: Afghanistan ungoverned by Britain, its internal politics unresolved, its relationship with the Sikhs unchanged. Dost Mohammad is eventually released and returned to Kabul, where he governs until 1863. The lessons drawn in London are the same lessons that later British, Soviet, and American planners will fail to apply. Afghanistan cannot be controlled from outside. Every army that tries will eventually leave — and leave the same way.
