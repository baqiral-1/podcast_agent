The Anarchy

It began as a trading company. It ended as the most powerful military force on the subcontinent. In the space of a few decades, a London corporation with shareholders and quarterly reports conquered an empire of 200 million people. They toppled emperors, engineered famines, looted treasuries, and fought wars for profit. From the counting houses of the City to the burning plains of India, this is the story of how corporate greed devoured a civilization, and how the men who ran the machine were finally called to account. The reckoning, when it came, shook Parliament to its foundations.

Episode 1

Origins: Founders' Hall and the Mughal Twilight

In 1599, while Shakespeare writes Hamlet just downriver, a group of London merchants hatch a scheme that will reshape the world. Their joint-stock venture starts small, trading pepper and spice, but ambition grows with every voyage. After brutal clashes with the Dutch, the Company pivots to India, establishing a glittering settlement at Madras. Meanwhile, the once-invincible Mughal Empire is cracking from within. When Nader Shah sacks Delhi in a bloodbath that stains the streets red, the door swings open for new predators. The Company is ready to walk through.

The East India Company was born in 1599 as a revolutionary experiment in corporate finance, a joint-stock venture that pooled the capital of London merchants hungry for the riches of the spice trade. Founded while Shakespeare labored over Hamlet just downriver, the Company began modestly, sending ships to Southeast Asia to compete with the dominant Dutch. Those early encounters were bruising: the Dutch were ruthless competitors who controlled the Spice Islands with fortified trading posts and superior naval power. Driven out of the most lucrative Eastern markets, the Company made a pivotal decision to redirect its energies toward the Indian subcontinent. The settlement at Madras became its first major foothold, a fortified coastal enclave where English traders operated under Mughal sufferance. For decades the arrangement worked, as the Company remained a minor player in a subcontinent dominated by the vast and wealthy Mughal Empire. But the Mughal system was rotting from within. Provincial governors became de facto independent rulers. Court intrigues paralyzed central authority. Then came the catastrophe: in 1739, Nader Shah of Persia invaded, sacking Delhi with such apocalyptic violence that drains ran with blood for days. The massacre destroyed the Mughal mystique of invincibility and opened India to a free-for-all among competing powers. Anglo-French rivalry was already laying a trail of gunpowder across India's contested southern coastline, and the Company was quietly transforming from humble trader into something far more dangerous.

Episode 2

Conquest of Bengal: Plassey and the Diwani

Robert Clive arrives in India as a troubled young clerk and reinvents himself as a military adventurer with terrifying ambition. When the young Nawab of Bengal threatens the Company's interests, Clive hatches a conspiracy involving forged treaties, treacherous generals, and a battlefield where most of the enemy army simply refuses to fight. Plassey is less a battle than a corporate coup. The puppet nawabs that follow are worse than useless. Then comes Buxar, and with it, the prize that changes everything: the right to collect Bengal's vast revenues.

Robert Clive's conspiracy against Siraj ud-Daula culminated at Plassey in 1757, a battle that was really a corporate takeover disguised as warfare. Clive had secretly bribed Mir Jafar, the Nawab's commanding general, to betray his master on the battlefield. When the moment came, most of the Bengali army simply stood and watched as Clive's smaller force attacked. Siraj fled and was murdered, and Clive installed Mir Jafar as a pliant puppet. The looting that followed was extraordinary: Clive personally amassed a fortune that would make him one of the richest men in England. But puppet nawabs proved useless administrators. Mir Jafar's ineffectual rule left soldiers unpaid and Murshidabad crumbling, while Delhi suffered its own cascading decline. His replacement, Mir Qasim, proved dangerously competent, clashing openly with Company officials over trade privileges and revenue. When diplomacy failed, Mir Qasim formed a desperate alliance with Shuja ud-Daula, the Nawab of Avadh, and Shah Alam, the fugitive Mughal Emperor. Their combined forces met the Company at Buxar in 1764, and the result was decisive: a shattering British victory that broke the last organized opposition in eastern India. In the aftermath came the Diwani, the Company's most consequential prize. This Mughal grant gave a London trading corporation the legal right to collect revenues across Bengal, Bihar, and Orissa, territories containing thirty million people. A commercial enterprise was now effectively governing one of the richest provinces on earth.

Episode 3

Famine, Scandal, and the Desolation of Delhi

The monsoon of 1768 fails, and Bengal starves. Ten million people die while Company officials continue extracting revenue from the dying. In London, the stock price collapses and Parliament demands answers. Warren Hastings arrives to reform the wreckage, but finds himself locked in a vicious power struggle with his own council member, Philip Francis. Far to the south, Company armies meet a devastating defeat at Pollilur. And in Delhi, a blinded emperor sits helpless as a sadistic warlord storms the Red Fort. The horror of what happens next defies belief.

The Bengal famine of 1770 was a man-amplified catastrophe of staggering proportions. When the monsoon failed in 1768, the Company's relentless revenue extraction turned a drought into a death sentence for millions. Fields lay barren, villages emptied, and corpses choked the rivers. Yet Company officials continued demanding taxes from the survivors. In London, the financial fallout nearly destroyed the Company itself, triggering a stock collapse and forcing Parliament to intervene for the first time. Warren Hastings arrived as governor with a mandate to reform, but the reality on the ground was far more complex. His bitter rivalry with Philip Francis on the Calcutta council escalated into duels, conspiracies, and paralysis at the highest levels of Company administration. Meanwhile, the myth of British military invincibility was shattered in southern India, where Hyder Ali's forces annihilated a Company army at Pollilur in one of the most devastating defeats Europeans had suffered in India. The cavalry charge came so fast that soldiers barely had time to form squares before they were overwhelmed. Far to the north, the situation in Delhi had descended into nightmare. Shah Alam, the Mughal Emperor who had granted the Company its precious Diwani, attempted to reclaim his capital with Maratha support. But the Marathas proved unreliable allies, and when the Afghan-backed warlord Ghulam Qadir stormed the Red Fort, the emperor was subjected to cruelties almost beyond description. The blinding of the emperor in his own palace marked the final degradation of Mughal authority, turning the once-mighty dynasty into a symbol of helpless victimhood.

Episode 4

Impeachment, Final Conquests, and Corporate Legacy

Warren Hastings returns to London expecting a hero's welcome. Instead, Edmund Burke drags him before Parliament in the most spectacular political trial of the century. For seven years, the impeachment exposes the Company's crimes before a horrified public. But while London debates, the Company keeps conquering. Tipu Sultan falls at Seringapatam. The Marathas are crushed. Delhi itself is seized. A corporation born to trade pepper now rules an empire of 200 million souls. The question that haunts us: how did the world let it happen?

The impeachment of Warren Hastings in 1788 was the eighteenth century's most dramatic political spectacle. Edmund Burke, in speeches that lasted days, laid bare the Company's record of extortion, famine, and broken treaties before a packed Westminster Hall. The trial became a national reckoning with the moral cost of empire, as Burke argued that no amount of profit could justify the suffering inflicted on millions. Hastings was ultimately acquitted after seven exhausting years, but the damage to the Company's reputation was profound. Parliament imposed new regulations, and the fiction that the Company was merely a trading concern was forever shattered. Yet none of this slowed the machine of conquest. In southern India, Tipu Sultan had emerged as the Company's most formidable adversary, allying with revolutionary France and modernizing his army along European lines. His death at the fall of Seringapatam in 1799 removed the last great Indian power capable of challenging British expansion. The Marathas, weakened by internal divisions, fell in a series of campaigns that left no indigenous military force standing. Lord Wellesley's ambitions extended even to Delhi itself, where the blind Mughal Emperor was reduced to a pensioner of the Company, his authority surviving only as a useful legal fiction. By the early nineteenth century, a joint-stock corporation headquartered in a London counting house controlled territories stretching from the Himalayas to Cape Comorin, governing more people than any European nation. The legacy of corporate empire would shape the subcontinent for centuries to come.
