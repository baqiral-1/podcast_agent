The Anarchy

It was not a government. It was not an army. It was a private corporation that conquered a subcontinent. William Dalrymple's The Anarchy traces the East India Company from its founding in 1599 to the conquest of India — four episodes of corporate violence, systemic plunder, and the extraordinary story of how the world's first multinational corporation became the world's most powerful military state.

Episode 1

Origins of a Corporate Empire

In 1599, London merchants invent the joint-stock company. One hundred and sixty years later, it owns an empire.

December 31, 1600. A charter signed by Elizabeth I gives a group of London merchants the right to trade with the East Indies. What makes this unusual is the financial instrument underneath it: joint-stock capital, allowing merchants who never sail or bargain in bazaars to invest, see their shares trade, and profit from ventures they have never witnessed. The East India Company is born. The episode follows the Company from its first tentative factories on the Indian coast to its emergence as a territorial power after the Battle of Plassey in 1757. In 1765, the Mughal Emperor Shah Alam signs the Diwani — granting the Company the right to collect revenue from Bengal, Bihar, and Orissa. The signing takes place on Robert Clive's dining-room table. The Mughal Emperor, reduced to a piece of furniture's width of dignity, grants imperial authority to a trading company from London. The episode ends with a question that will define the next hundred years: what does a corporation do when it discovers that governing is more profitable than trading?

Episode 2

Plassey and the Seizure of Bengal

Clive's army is outnumbered four to one at Plassey. He wins anyway — through betrayal, fog, and a secret arrangement with the enemy's commander.

June 1756. Siraj ud-Daula, the Nawab of Bengal, moves on Calcutta with seventy thousand troops. Fort William's garrison of 265 soldiers panics and flees to boats; many drown in the chaos. The Nawab takes the fort. Robert Clive arrives from Madras with a relief force, retakes Calcutta, and then turns on the French at Chandernagar. The episode climaxes at Plassey, June 23, 1757, where Clive's smaller force faces Siraj's army across the Bhagirathi River. Victory comes not through superior firepower but through a secret arrangement with Mir Jafar, one of Siraj's senior commanders, who has agreed to hold his forces back in exchange for the nawabship. The battle is decided before it is fought. Siraj flees, is captured, and is killed. Mir Jafar becomes nawab. The Company becomes the power behind the throne of Bengal — the richest province in the subcontinent — and begins extracting its wealth with a thoroughness that no previous ruler had attempted.

Episode 3

Famine, Imperial Ruin, and Moral Reckoning

The monsoon fails. Bengal starves. The Company's revenue goes up.

1769. The monsoon fails. The Ganges shrinks to mud. The Bengal famine begins — and the Company, which now governs Bengal, faces a choice between administration and extraction. It chooses extraction. While farmers cannot sow and laborers sell their children, the Company maintains its tax collection and protects its military budget. By February 1770, rice prices have risen tenfold. Estimates suggest 1.2 million deaths — some say more. The Company reports to London that tax revenues have increased. Warren Hastings arrives as governor-general, attempting administrative reforms while battling Philip Francis, the parliamentary overseer who immediately becomes his enemy. Shah Alam marches from Allahabad toward Delhi hoping to restore Mughal authority. The march is ceremonially successful and politically meaningless. The episode traces how corporate governance without accountability produces a logic of extraction that registers human catastrophe as a line item — neutral, necessary, beneath comment — while the shareholders in London count their dividends.

Episode 4

Wellesley, Tipu Sultan, and Paramountcy

Wellesley arrives in Calcutta with orders to secure India for Britain. Tipu Sultan dies fighting on the ramparts of his own fort.

1798. Richard Wellesley arrives as Governor-General with explicit instructions from William Pitt: expel French influence from India and secure the subcontinent for Britain. He moves first against Hyderabad, surrounding French-trained forces in their own cantonment overnight and disarming fourteen thousand men without firing a shot. Then he turns to Mysore. Tipu Sultan — the 'Tiger of Mysore,' Islam's most committed adversary of British power — is surrounded at Seringapatam. Harris's army of 100,000 camp followers and 30,000 troops advances with overwhelming artillery. On May 4, 1799, British stormers breach the walls. Tipu, wounded twice by bayonet and once by musket ball, receives a final shot through the temple at point-blank range. He dies on the ramparts of his own fort. His body is found in the darkness, eyes still open. The episode closes with the question that his death raises and the Company will spend the next fifty years evading: what happens when there is nobody left to conquer?
