India After Gandhi

They said India would not survive. That it was too large, too diverse, too wounded by partition to hold together as a democracy. They were wrong — but barely. Ramachandra Guha's epic chronicle follows ten episodes of the world's most improbable republic: independence, Kashmir, Emergency, assassination, liberalization, and the stubborn insistence of 350 million first-time voters that they, too, had something to say about who governed them.

Episode 1

Partition and the Problem of India

August 15, 1947. A governor holds a party while his city burns. An empire ends in fire and blood.

August 15, 1947. The new governor of West Punjab holds a party in Government House in Lahore as the city burns around him — fires rising into darkness, electricity failed, gunfire echoing through streets empty of everyone but the fleeing and the dead. This is what independence looks like in the middle of Partition. The episode traces the political deadlock that made partition seem inevitable: Jinnah's demand for Pakistan, Nehru's vision of unity, Mountbatten's accelerated timeline. Then Gandhi's assassination in January 1948 — the first great shock of the new republic. Yet something remarkable happens: India does not splinter. Despite prophets of failure in every capital, the republic holds together not through unanimity but through the oldest of democratic instruments — argument. The first leaders of an improbable nation set out to build a state from scratch with a partition wound still fresh, a treasury nearly empty, and millions of people who have never voted, never been asked what they think.

Episode 2

Integration, Kashmir, and Refugee Reconstruction

Patel integrates 562 princely states by persuasion and pressure. Kashmir needs an airlift.

Sardar Patel has an extraordinary task: fold 562 princely states — each with its own ruler, its own army, its own relationship with the British Crown — into the new Indian republic. He does it, mostly, through a combination of persuasion, financial incentives, and the implicit threat of a new national army. The geography cooperates. The politics barely do. Kashmir is the exception. The Hindu Maharaja of a Muslim-majority territory delays his decision as Pakistani-backed tribal raiders advance on Srinagar. India airlifts troops on October 27, 1947 — one of the first military airlifts in history — and holds the airport by hours. The episode also follows millions of refugees crossing the new borders in both directions, rebuilding lives with nothing but what they carried. Sindhis who lost their homeland to Pakistan reconstruct entire communities within Indian territory. The administrative stamina required to absorb this catastrophe without institutional collapse is itself a kind of miracle.

Episode 3

Constitution, Franchise, and Non-Alignment

India grants voting rights to 350 million people, most of them illiterate. This has never been attempted.

The Constituent Assembly, led by Dr. B.R. Ambedkar, debates the structure of the new republic. One decision above all others defines India's founding audacity: universal adult franchise, immediately, for every citizen regardless of education, property, or caste. Three hundred and fifty million people. Most of them illiterate. Most of them voting for the first time. It has never been attempted at this scale anywhere on earth. In Himachal Pradesh, Buddhist villagers cast ballots before winter snow closes their mountain passes — some of the first votes in the history of the world's largest democracy. Nehru articulates non-alignment: India will refuse to choose between Washington and Moscow, will develop its own model, will represent the post-colonial world's aspiration for independence. The Kashmir conflict reaches the United Nations. India's first international crisis — a territorial dispute over mountains few outsiders have ever seen — becomes a frozen wound that will not thaw for seventy years.

Episode 4

Remaking Society: States, Development, Reform

A man fasts for fifty-eight days demanding a Telugu state. He dies. The map of India changes.

Potti Sriramulu fasts for fifty-eight days demanding a separate state for Telugu speakers. When he dies, mass unrest erupts — offices attacked, trains damaged, cities paralyzed. The central government, which had resisted linguistic reorganization as a threat to national unity, abruptly reverses course. If language defines community, and community demands representation, then the map of India must be redrawn along linguistic lines rather than colonial boundaries. This decision reshapes the entire federation. Nehru's development vision is also taking form: massive dams, steel plants, irrigation projects — the 'temples of modern India,' as he calls them. The Hindu Code Bill triggers fierce parliamentary combat over women's rights, marriage law, and caste inheritance. India does something most post-colonial nations avoid: it legislates radical social reform simultaneously with political constitution-building. Not perfectly. Not completely. But with enough force to begin transforming what a billion people can expect from their own government.

Episode 5

Regional Crises of the Union

The Bombay crisis pits Marathi speakers against Hindi speakers. Neither side blinks.

The linguistic reorganization of states doesn't resolve the questions of belonging — it multiplies them. The battle over Bombay becomes explosively tense: Marathi speakers demand it as the capital of a new Maharashtra state; Gujarati speakers want it as a joint city; Hindi nationalists want it as nobody's capital at all. Meanwhile, in the northeast, the Naga hills slide into bitter insurgency. The Nagas — ethnically distinct, largely Christian, never fully integrated into British India — resist incorporation into a Hindu-majority republic. India's first hidden war begins: counterinsurgency in mountains few Indians have ever visited. In Kerala in 1957, communists win a state election — the first democratic communist victory anywhere in the world. The result alarms the Congress establishment and the Church in equal measure. President's Rule is imposed. An elected government is dismissed. India's democracy reveals its contradictions: universal franchise does not guarantee universal results will be honored.

Episode 6

Nehru's Last Years and Secular India's Tests

The Dalai Lama crosses into India. China takes note. Then China attacks.

March 1959. The Dalai Lama flees Tibet across the Himalayas and arrives in India as a refugee. Nehru grants asylum. China, which had been cultivating India as a model of post-colonial solidarity, regards the gesture as betrayal. Diplomatic negotiations fail. In October 1962, Chinese forces pour across the disputed border in two simultaneous attacks. Indian troops, inadequately equipped and poorly deployed, collapse. The military humiliation is total. Nehru, visibly shaken and aging, never recovers. He dies in May 1964, and with him dies a certain confidence in the idea that India could shape its own destiny through moral authority alone. Succession becomes chaotic. The 'Syndicate' — Congress party bosses — chooses Lal Bahadur Shastri as a manageable compromise. Shastri surprises everyone by standing firm in the 1965 war with Pakistan. Then he dies at the peace negotiations in Tashkent. The path now opens, unexpectedly, for Jawaharlal Nehru's daughter.

Episode 7

Post-Nehru Upheaval and Indira's Ascent

The party bosses think they can control Indira Gandhi. They are wrong.

Indira Gandhi inherits power as a compromise candidate the Congress Syndicate expects to manage. They will spend years discovering their mistake. She fights her own party for control, splits Congress in 1969, and wins a massive mandate in the 1971 general election on the slogan 'Garibi Hatao' — abolish poverty. Weeks later, East Pakistan erupts in mass violence as the Pakistani military suppresses Bengali nationalism with extraordinary brutality. Ten million refugees cross into India. Indira Gandhi commits India's military. In December 1971, the Pakistani army in the east surrenders to Indian forces in under two weeks. Bangladesh is born. Indira returns to Delhi as a conquering hero — Time magazine compares her to Durga, the Hindu goddess of war. The adulation creates its own dangers. A leader who has known real power — and who has seen rivals try to remove her — begins to treat threats to her position as threats to the nation.

Episode 8

Emergency and Democratic Recovery

Democracy is suspended. Sanjay Gandhi runs the country from the back seat. Then something unexpected happens.

June 25, 1975. President Fakhruddin Ali Ahmed, acting on Indira Gandhi's advice, declares a state of Emergency. Opposition leaders are arrested overnight. The press is censored. Fundamental rights are suspended. For twenty-one months, Indira governs without parliament and Sanjay Gandhi — her younger son, with no elected office — runs coercive social campaigns: forced sterilizations, slum demolitions, urban beautification programs that destroy the livelihoods of the poor. Jayaprakash Narayan, the aging Gandhian opposition leader, is imprisoned. Yet something underground persists: civil resistance, pamphlets, the memory of what democracy was supposed to mean. In 1977, Indira calls elections, confident of victory. Instead, Indians vote her out — the first transfer of power away from Congress since independence. The Janata coalition takes office. It soon fractures and fails. But its brief existence proves something essential: Indian democracy has roots deeper than any individual leader, deeper even than the party that claimed to have created it.

Episode 9

Fragmentation, Identity Politics, and the End of One-Party India

Operation Bluestar. Then a knock at the back door of the Prime Minister's house.

Punjab is burning. Sikh militants, led by the charismatic and volatile Jarnail Singh Bhindranwale, have turned the Golden Temple in Amritsar into an armed stronghold. Demands for Khalistan — a separate Sikh homeland — escalate into assassinations, bombings, and the systematic killing of Hindus. Indira Gandhi orders Operation Bluestar: the Indian army storms the Golden Temple in June 1984. Bhindranwale dies in the fighting. The temple is damaged. The operation succeeds tactically. Its consequences are catastrophic. On October 31, 1984, Indira Gandhi is shot by two of her Sikh bodyguards in the garden of her own residence. Anti-Sikh violence erupts across India in the days that follow — organized, brutal, and largely unpunished. Thousands die. But the republic does not collapse: elections are called, a new government takes power, and democratic processes resume. India survives its worst political crisis since Partition through the same instrument that always saves it — the vote.

Episode 10

Hindutva, Liberalization, and the Bargaining Republic

A sixteenth-century mosque is demolished by a crowd. India survives that too.

December 6, 1992. A Hindu nationalist crowd demolishes the Babri Mosque in Ayodhya — a sixteenth-century structure that, they claim, marks the birthplace of Ram. The demolition is broadcast live. Communal riots sweep India in the weeks that follow, killing thousands in Bombay, Surat, Delhi. The secular promise of the republic seems, for a moment, to have been broken beyond repair. It holds, narrowly. Meanwhile, Rajiv Gandhi's economic liberalization has already begun India's transformation: satellite television, mobile phones, software exports, a new middle class that shops in malls and argues in English on the internet. Two Indias coexist in the same decade — a village India still governed by panchayats and seasonal debt, and a Bangalore India of air-conditioned offices and stock market dreams. The republic holds both, imperfectly, argumentatively, through elections that nobody fully controls. Not quite a democracy. Not quite failing. Something stranger and more durable: a bargaining republic that will not stop bargaining.
