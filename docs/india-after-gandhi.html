India After Gandhi

Stories and analyses adapted from Ramachandra Guha's history of modern India.

Episode 1

Birth in Fire: Partition, Integration, and the Making of the Republic

Partition violence, refugee columns, and the rush to build a new state define India’s first years of freedom.

Independence arrives under fire. Lahore burns on the night of 15 August 1947 as Partition unleashes massacres, refugee columns, and the largest displacement in modern South Asian history. The episode follows the first years of the republic: the emergency of refugee resettlement, the integration of princely states, and the struggle to keep India unified while Kashmir erupts and Hyderabad resists. Gandhi’s assassination tests the new state’s moral claim, while Patel and Nehru race to stitch a shattered map together. We end with the Constitution and the first general election, a leap of faith that makes universal franchise real in a poor and traumatized country. The republic is not born whole; it is built through crisis, improvisation, and a decision to trust ordinary citizens with power.

Episode 2

Nehru’s India: Development, Non-Alignment, and the Recasting of the Union

Linguistic states, planning, and non-alignment define a confident decade that ends in a hard border war.

Potti Sriramulu’s death forces Nehru to create Andhra and opens the era of linguistic states, redrawing India’s internal map from below. The States Reorganisation Commission and the creation of Andhra become a template, while Nehru projects non-alignment at Bandung and the United Nations. The episode tracks the Nehruvian project: planning commissions, dams and steel plants, non-alignment abroad, and a tightrope between idealism and hard power. We move from Kashmir and Pakistan to the emerging conflict with China, where diplomatic confidence collides with a brutal border war in 1962. Inside India, Congress dominance masks regional pressures, while the state expands education, industry, and bureaucracy. The Nehru years leave a paradoxical legacy: a democratic and developmental vision that enlarges the Union, even as external shocks and internal demands begin to fray its certainties.

Episode 3

After Nehru: Succession, Populism, and the Emergency State

Succession battles and mass politics lead to the 1975 Emergency and the suspension of civil liberties.

After the 1962 China war, Nehru’s authority ebbs and succession politics takes over. Shastri’s brief tenure, the 1965 war, and Indira Gandhi’s rise set the stage for a more personal style of rule. The episode follows the 1967 electoral shock, factional battles inside Congress, and the populist turn that culminates in the 1971 victory and a centralized executive. The Bangladesh war and the rhetoric of Garibi Hatao deepen Indira’s authority even as institutions strain. By 1975, economic strain and mass protests meet a court verdict that threatens Indira’s seat. The response is the Emergency: press censorship, arrests, and the suspension of civil liberties. The narrative shows how a constitutional democracy chose coercion over compromise and how that choice reshaped Indian politics for decades.

Episode 4

Democracy Unsettled: Post-Congress Politics, Identity, and Violence

After the Emergency, India becomes more competitive and more fragmented, with rising regional and communal tensions.

The Emergency’s violence is captured in Turkman Gate, where bulldozers, tear gas, and bullets cleared a Delhi neighborhood in the name of order. From there the episode traces the post-Emergency landscape: the Janata experiment, Congress’s return, and a democracy that becomes more competitive yet more fragmented. Regional parties rise and state governments become the arena for experimentation and conflict. We follow the rise of identity politics and the spread of coercive state power into Punjab, Kashmir, and the Northeast, alongside communal riots that test the secular promise of the republic. The story is one of contradiction: widening participation and local power on the one hand, and recurring episodes of repression on the other. India’s institutions survive, but the social fabric is repeatedly strained by violence, impunity, and the politics of fear.

Episode 5

New India? Coalition Rule, Liberalization, and Popular Culture

Coalition politics, market reforms, and mass media remake public life without resolving India’s inequalities.

Coalition rule replaces one-party dominance as the economy opens to liberalization. The episode moves from the Mandal era and the rise of the BJP to the reforms of the 1990s, tracking how market growth and globalization coexist with stubborn hunger and inequality. We see politics recalibrated around region, caste, and media spectacle, while satellite television and Bollywood knit together a sprawling, multilingual public. The narrative follows the country into the Vajpayee years, with technology, consumer culture, and diaspora influence rising alongside communal tension. Economic growth accelerates, but rural distress and corruption keep the political mood volatile. India is neither the failed state of pessimists nor the superpower of glossy headlines. It is louder, richer, more democratic, more unequal, and harder to govern — a complex republic still arguing about who it is.
