India After Gandhi

Five episodes trace India’s post-1947 arc from Partition and constitution-making through Nehru’s state-building, Indira’s Emergency, and the turn to coalition politics. The story ends with liberalization, identity movements, and mass culture remaking the republic.

Episode 1

Partition, Integration, and the Democratic Founding

Partition’s wounds, integration of princely states, and the constitution test a newborn democracy.

India’s independence begins with Partition’s wound. This episode tracks the refugee crisis, the assassination of Gandhi, and the frantic effort to keep a vast, exhausted country from splintering. Sardar Patel and V.P. Menon move to integrate hundreds of princely states, while Nehru tries to balance idealism with the raw demands of security and food. Hyderabad’s defiance shows how quickly force can replace persuasion. The constitution is drafted to hold together a nation of languages, religions, and castes; the question is whether democracy can survive the very violence that birthed it. Kashmir’s accession becomes the first international test, and the army’s role in a civilian republic is defined in real time. By the time India’s first general election arrives, the new state has survived its first shocks, but the shape of federalism, minority protection, and national unity is still unsettled. Independence is declared; the argument over what it means has only begun.

Episode 2

Nehru’s Republic: Planning, Federalism, and Border Strains

Planning, linguistic states, and border wars test Nehru’s developmental vision.

Nehru’s India tries to turn freedom into a functioning state. The episode moves from the Planning Commission and the choice of a mixed economy to the struggle over language, federal boundaries, and the place of the countryside in a nation of cities and factories. Dams rise as temples of modernity, but harvests and prices keep politics uneasy. The Congress party dominates elections, yet regional movements and linguistic agitations force Delhi to redraw the map. Abroad, nonalignment promises autonomy, but the borders are porous and contested: Pakistan remains a rival, and China’s claims in the Himalayas harden. We follow the slow building of institutions—courts, bureaucracy, and a public sector—while shortages and corruption test the promise of socialism. When the 1962 war with China shatters Nehru’s aura, the republic discovers how quickly a moral vision can meet the limits of geography. The question after defeat is whether the system he built can survive without him.

Episode 3

After Nehru: Defeat, Succession, and Indira’s Break

Shastri’s death and Indira’s rise end Congress consensus and begin personal rule.

Nehru’s death leaves a vacuum that the Congress party expects to control, but history refuses to cooperate. The episode moves through the uneasy Shastri years—food shortages, the 1965 war with Pakistan, and the search for a steady hand. Shastri’s sudden death in Tashkent opens the door to Indira, and the party chooses the wrong person to underestimate. Indira Gandhi rises from a compromise candidate to a leader with her own power base. Regional parties grow louder, caste coalitions shift, and the party’s old bosses find themselves outmaneuvered. The Green Revolution begins to change the countryside, promising abundance while deepening inequality. When Indira nationalizes banks and calls for a “garibi hatao” mandate, the Congress splits, and the politics of consensus gives way to an era of personal rule. By the end, India is no longer the quiet, gentlemanly democracy of the 1950s. The next question is how far that centralization will go.

Episode 4

Indira Ascendant: Victory, Emergency, and the Post-Congress Experiment

Bangladesh victory leads to Emergency rule and a fragile post-Congress experiment.

Indira’s dominance peaks with the 1971 war and the creation of Bangladesh, a victory that seems to settle her authority. Refugees pour in from East Pakistan, and the war cements her reputation as a decisive leader. But triumph becomes overreach. The memory of victory fades as dissent spreads. The episode follows economic strain, corruption scandals, and the rise of Jayaprakash Narayan’s movement as Indira declares the Emergency in 1975 after a court judgment threatens her mandate, suspending civil liberties, censoring the press, and jailing opponents. Sanjay Gandhi’s sterilization drives and slum demolitions turn the state’s modernizing zeal into fear. When elections are finally called in 1977, the opposition unites to defeat her, producing India’s first non-Congress government. The Janata coalition promises to restore democracy but struggles to govern a factional country. The episode ends with Indira sensing her route back, and the question of whether the Emergency’s lessons will actually change the republic.

Episode 5

From Rajiv to Liberalization: Identity, Coalition Rule, and Mass Culture

Assassination, liberalization, and identity politics remake India’s democracy.

Indira returns to power, only to confront Punjab’s insurgency and the Golden Temple crisis. Her assassination in 1984 brings Rajiv Gandhi to office, promising technocratic renewal but facing the Bofors scandal, ethnic violence, and the limits of a centralized state. The episode follows the opening of television and consumer culture, the Mandal Commission’s upheaval, and the rise of Hindu nationalism as the old Congress umbrella collapses. Kashmir’s insurgency and the Ayodhya dispute reveal how faith can mobilize votes and violence. Coalition governments become the new normal, with regional parties bargaining for power in Delhi. Economic crisis in 1991 forces a break with the old model, launching liberalization that reshapes cities, jobs, and aspirations. By the end, India is louder, more prosperous, and more divided, with identity politics and market forces both pulling at the same fabric. The question is whether democratic pluralism can keep pace with the speed of change.
