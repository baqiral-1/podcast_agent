Freedom at Midnight

Four episodes follow the British exit from India, the scramble to draw borders, and the violence of Partition. From Mountbatten’s rushed timetable to Gandhi’s final fast and assassination, the series asks how independence became a tragedy as well as a triumph.

Episode 1

Empire in Retreat

An exhausted empire, a shrinking timetable, and irreconcilable demands push Partition from threat to plan.

Britain’s war-ravaged empire limps toward the exit, but India’s future is anything but agreed. Congress leaders press for a unified state; the Muslim League demands Pakistan; communal violence in Calcutta and Noakhali foreshadows what politics cannot contain. We follow Gandhi’s lonely marches for peace, the failure of the Cabinet Mission, and the steady collapse of British authority as mutiny becomes a whisper and famine a memory that still haunts the streets. Attlee’s government wants out, Wavell is spent, and Jinnah senses leverage in every delay. Into this disarray steps Lord Mountbatten, sent to engineer a transfer of power at speed. He finds a timetable that keeps shrinking, a map no one can draw without blood, and princes and Sikhs who fear being stranded on the wrong side of history. Mountbatten accelerates the schedule, promising August 1947, and no one sleeps. As the deadline moves closer, one question dominates: can independence arrive without catastrophe?

Episode 2

The Bargain to Divide India

Radcliffe’s commission draws a line through Punjab and Bengal as leaders accept a bitter compromise.

Mountbatten’s plan turns argument into arithmetic: who keeps which districts, who controls Calcutta, how to split Punjab’s canals and railways. The episode follows the high-wire bargaining between Congress, the Muslim League, and the departing British as the idea of partition hardens into a map. Nehru and Patel accept a bitter compromise, betting unity is no longer salvageable. Cyril Radcliffe arrives with no time and no local knowledge, presiding over boundary commissions that argue from statistics and fear. Sikh leaders demand a homeland, Bengal’s politics fracture, and provincial governors try to keep order while the deadline moves closer. Delhi rehearses a pageant of freedom even as trains begin to carry families in the wrong direction. The closer the line gets to the ground, the more lives it will cut across. When the final award is sealed in secret, only one thing is certain: the map will arrive after the violence has already begun.

Episode 3

Midnight Freedom, Massacre, and Kashmir

Independence dawns amid massacre, refugee columns, and a Kashmir crisis that threatens a new war.

Independence comes at midnight, but the streets are on fire. As India and Pakistan are born, the Radcliffe Line lands like a guillotine, splitting villages, canals, and police forces. Delhi celebrates while the countryside burns. In Lahore and Amritsar, neighbor turns on neighbor while hastily drawn borders turn trains into moving battlegrounds. The episode follows the spiraling violence in Punjab and Bengal, the refugee columns that clog the roads, and the frantic attempts to restore order with shaky new armies. Gandhi refuses to celebrate, walking through riot-torn neighborhoods and fasting to shame leaders into peace. Meanwhile princely states weigh their choices; Hyderabad resists, and Kashmir’s ruler hesitates as armed men cross the borders. The new governments discover that sovereignty is an empty word without safety, and that the first test of freedom is whether it can protect the weakest. The episode ends with Kashmir on the edge and both nations pulled toward war.

Episode 4

The Last Fast and the Assassination

Gandhi’s last fast, princely-state coercion, and an assassin’s bullet redefine the new republic.

Independence does not end the crisis. In Delhi, Gandhi launches a final fast, demanding that Hindu and Muslim leaders stop the killings and honor their pledges. His hunger turns into the last moral bargaining chip of the new republic, forcing cabinet ministers, RSS leaders, and refugee mobs to sign fragile truce agreements. Patel and Menon push to integrate the princely states; Hyderabad becomes the test case for coercion in a country still bleeding. Kashmir’s war drags on, and Delhi fills with refugees who want justice, not patience. Meanwhile Pakistan grapples with its own birth pains and Karachi’s uncertainty. The episode traces how the moral authority of one man collides with the machinery of a state that needs to look strong. Then, on a January evening in 1948, a bullet ends the fast and shatters the fragile calm. The nation mourns, but the political future is already tilting toward a harder, more anxious kind of unity.
