God's Shadow

In 1492, the year Columbus sailed, an Ottoman prince named Selim was watching from a distant frontier — and the world he would inherit would stretch from the Atlantic to the Indian Ocean. Alan Mikhail's God's Shadow traces the sultan who shaped the modern world before modernity had a name: three episodes that move from dynastic warfare to global conquest, from the shores of the Caribbean to the holy cities of Mecca and Medina.

Episode 1

Selim's World and the Atlantic Turn

An Ottoman prince dies in French captivity. His brother watches from a Black Sea frontier. The world is about to change.

Naples, February 1495. An Ottoman prince named Cem lies dying in the care of a French king's physician — thirteen years a captive, used as political leverage by Christian rulers who hoped his existence could destabilize his brother's throne. He dies at thirty-five, his body failing, his usefulness exhausted. Six hundred miles away, young Selim is being trained in an Ottoman system where only one son survives succession. This episode traces the rise of the Ottoman Empire from Anatolian obscurity to Mediterranean dominance after the 1453 conquest of Constantinople — and introduces the parallel world of 1492: Spain expelling Muslims and Jews, Columbus sailing west, Europe's religious purifications creating the refugees that the Ottoman world will absorb. Two empires. Two systems. One offering expulsion and one offering refuge. Selim is watching, learning, and waiting. The world he inherits will be shaped by the collision between them.

Episode 2

1492's Aftershocks: Slavery, Heresy, Road to Power

Muslim slaves in the Caribbean revolt against their Spanish masters. The Ottoman court welcomes the exiles Spain expelled.

The same year Spain creates its purified Christian order, enslaved Wolof Muslims and Taino peoples in Hispaniola join together and march on Santo Domingo — one of history's earliest colonial slave rebellions, erased from most histories. Meanwhile, Sephardic Jews expelled from Spain arrive in Ottoman ports, bringing skills in medicine, munitions, banking, and diplomacy. Jewish families like the Hamons rise to positions of trust in the imperial court. Selim, governing Trabzon on the Black Sea frontier, administers a territory of religious coexistence while his rivals plot in Istanbul. The episode traces the profound contrast: European expansion built on racial slavery and religious purification, Ottoman expansion built on incorporation and pragmatic tolerance. Then the political crisis begins. Safavid religious influence spreads through Anatolia, attracting Ottoman subjects toward a rival ideology. Selim begins building alliances in Crimea. The path to power is opening — but it will require removing his own father.

Episode 3

Conquest, Cairo, and Selim's Legacy

Selim defeats his father, destroys his brothers, conquers Egypt — then renames the world.

Selim moves. He deposes his aging father Bayezit in a coup, eliminates his brothers, and emerges as sole sultan. Then he turns outward. At Chaldiran in 1514, superior Ottoman artillery and cavalry shatter the Safavid army, securing the empire's eastern frontier. Three years later, Selim turns south toward Egypt. The Mamluk Sultanate, controller of the holy cities of Mecca and Medina, meets him at Ridaniya. Betrayal within the Mamluk high command shatters their resistance. Cairo falls. Selim takes the treasury, the relics of the Prophet, the title Protector of the Two Holy Cities. The Ottoman Empire now spans from the Atlantic to the Indian Ocean. And Selim, standing before a world map, begins to rename it — changing Italian and Latin inscriptions into Ottoman Turkish. Constantinople becomes Istanbul. Egypt becomes Mısır. The gesture is not merely territorial. It is the act of a man who believes the world belongs to him, and who has the power to make that belief stick.
