The Fall of the Ottomans

The Ottoman Empire entered the First World War hoping to recover what it had lost. What it found was annihilation. Eugene Rogan's The Fall of the Ottomans traces five episodes of the Great War in the Middle East — from the desperate alliance with Germany to genocide, Gallipoli, Kut, and the final collapse — revealing how a six-hundred-year empire ended in four years of catastrophic war.

Episode 1

Revolution, Ruin, and the Road to War

At a dinner party, Enver Pasha announces that two German warships have entered Ottoman waters. The empire is now in the war.

August 10, 1914. A dinner party at the Grand Vizier's residence. Enver Pasha, Minister of War and the most powerful member of the Committee of Union and Progress, announces that the German warships Goeben and Breslau have entered Ottoman waters. Under international law, a neutral state must either expel foreign warships within twenty-four hours or intern them. The German ambassador refuses internment. Ottoman officials improvise a solution: a fictitious purchase, a renaming — the ships become the Yavuz Sultan Selim and Medilli, their German crews become Ottoman sailors in Ottoman uniforms. The empire has entered a war it did not choose and is not ready to fight. The episode traces the catastrophic series of conflicts that preceded this moment — the 1911 war with Italy, the Balkan Wars of 1912-13, the loss of most Ottoman territory in Europe — and the desperation that drove the Young Turk government to seek a German alliance as the only path toward recovery. Every rescue has required another bargain. This one will cost everything.

Episode 2

Opening Campaigns: Jihad, Caucasus, Dardanelles

Enver Pasha marches an army into the Anatolian winter. The Bouvet sinks in two minutes.

November 1914. The Ottomans declare jihad — a call to holy war against Britain, France, and Russia that resonates across the colonial world but produces little practical military result. On the Caucasus front, Enver Pasha leads an ambitious offensive toward Sarıkamış in the dead of winter. The campaign is catastrophic: confusion, exposure, and disease destroy the Ottoman Third Army before it reaches its objectives. Hospitals overflow; men freeze in the mountains. In the south, a canal attack across Sinai fails against British defenses. But at the Dardanelles, the empire achieves an extraordinary defensive success. On March 18, 1915, a joint British-French naval force attempts to force the straits. Hidden mines tear open three battleships; the Bouvet rolls and sinks in two minutes, taking most of her crew. The Allied fleet withdraws, unable to believe that geography — mines, currents, shore batteries — has stopped the most powerful Mediterranean fleet afloat. The straits hold. For now.

Episode 3

1915: Genocide and Gallipoli

Armenian priest Grigoris Balakian hears a knock on his door late at night. By morning, 240 notables have been arrested.

April 24, 1915. Police arrive at the door of Armenian priest Grigoris Balakian. By dawn, 240 Armenian community leaders in Constantinople have been arrested. The arrests mark the beginning of what becomes systematic deportation and mass murder. The Young Turk government has declared the Armenian population a security threat and mobilizes state apparatus to deport them toward Syria and Iraq on roads that become killing grounds. Deportations harden into death marches. Estimates range from 600,000 to over one million killed. Simultaneously, Allied forces land at Gallipoli on April 25, opening the military campaign that dominates the year in the Western imagination. The episode traces the terrible duality of 1915: Ottoman military survival at Gallipoli — visible, photographed, celebrated — paired with systematic destruction inland that moves by decree and whisper. One struggle is a military campaign. The other is a genocide. Both happen in the same months, in the same empire, managed by the same government.

Episode 4

Mesopotamia, Kut, and the Arab Revolt

The armored steamer Julnar is caught on a cable stretched across the Tigris. It is the last hope of an entire army.

The armored steamer Julnar, loaded with supplies for the starving British garrison at Kut, approaches the Ottoman positions on the Tigris in the early hours of April 24, 1916. A cable has been stretched across the river. The Julnar cannot pass. The crew is captured. Kut surrenders four days later. General Townshend's garrison of 13,000 British and Indian troops has been besieged for 147 days. The prisoners are marched into captivity; hundreds die on the road. The fall of Kut is the worst British military defeat since Yorktown. As British forces absorb the disaster, the Arab Revolt begins. Sharif Husayn of Mecca, having received British promises of Arab independence, raises the banner of revolt against Ottoman rule in June 1916. His son Faisal transforms the uprising into a more disciplined force. T.E. Lawrence arrives in the Hijaz as British liaison. The revolt that follows will become legend. The episode ends with the empire fighting on two fronts it cannot fully defend.

Episode 5

Collapse, Armistice, and the New Middle East

General Maude enters Baghdad proclaiming liberation. The borders that follow will generate wars for the next century.

March 1917. General Stanley Maude enters Baghdad and issues a proclamation in English and Arabic: 'Our armies do not come into your cities and lands as conquerors or enemies, but as liberators.' The sentence is a masterpiece of imperial self-deception — and a template for every subsequent occupation of an Arab city by a Western army. Allenby takes Jerusalem in December. Damascus falls in October 1918. The Ottoman Empire requests an armistice. The episode traces what follows: a peace settlement built on wartime promises that contradict each other — the Husayn-McMahon correspondence, the Sykes-Picot Agreement, the Balfour Declaration — each committing the same territory to different futures. The borders imposed by the Paris peace settlements divide Kurds across four states, create Lebanon, partition Palestine, and invent Iraq. They are challenged almost immediately. They persist anyway, generating wars in 1948, 1967, 1973, and beyond. The Ottoman Empire has fallen. Its replacement is a map that will never quite work.
