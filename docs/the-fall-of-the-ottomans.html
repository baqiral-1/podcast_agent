The Fall of the Ottomans

An empire that ruled for six centuries is dragged into the deadliest conflict the world has ever seen. From revolutionary coups in Istanbul to frozen mountain passes, burning deserts, and genocidal death marches, the Ottoman front of the Great War reshapes the entire Middle East. Secret alliances, broken promises, and desperate gambles pit empires against each other while millions of soldiers and civilians pay the price. When the dust settles, ancient borders are erased, new nations are carved from the wreckage, and the consequences echo for a century. This is the story of how one war destroyed a world.

Episode 1

Revolution, Reform, and the Road to War

A paranoid sultan rules an empire in freefall. Then young revolutionaries seize power, promising democracy and salvation. But their reforms trigger a chain reaction: counter-revolution, ethnic massacre, and catastrophic wars in Libya and the Balkans that strip the empire of nearly all its European territories. As Europe's great powers arm themselves for a wider conflict, a secret alliance is struck in the dead of night. Two German warships steam toward Istanbul carrying the spark that will ignite a world war. The empire is about to make a gamble from which there is no return.

When Sultan Abdulhamid II was overthrown by idealistic young officers promising constitutional rule, it seemed like the dawn of a new era. Instead, it was the beginning of the end. The Young Turk revolution of 1908 unleashed forces no one could control: a counter-revolution crushed in blood, the massacre of thousands of Armenians in Adana, and a succession of devastating wars that cost the empire Libya, the Balkans, and nearly its survival. As the dust settled, a triumvirate of ambitious men seized absolute power, determined to restore Ottoman glory at any cost. But the world was changing faster than they could act. Italy invaded Libya. The Balkan states combined to drive the Ottomans from Europe. And in the shadows, German and British agents competed for the empire's allegiance. When a secret alliance was signed with Berlin and two German warships were welcomed into Istanbul under a fictive sale, the empire crossed a point of no return. Meanwhile, from Egypt to India to North Africa, millions of colonial subjects were being mobilized for a war they never chose. The road to the Great War was paved with broken promises, and the Ottoman Empire was about to discover the price of its gamble.

Episode 2

Opening Offensives: Sarikamish, Suez, and the Dardanelles

The empire launches three simultaneous offensives, each one a desperate gamble. In the frozen Caucasus mountains, an entire army vanishes in a blizzard. At the Suez Canal, soldiers cross the desert in silence, praying for a miracle. And at the Dardanelles, the world's most powerful navy steams toward the straits, convinced the Ottomans will crumble. They are all wrong. As Allied warships sink beneath Ottoman guns and thousands of soldiers wade ashore at Gallipoli into a wall of fire, a new kind of war is born, and no one is prepared for what comes next.

Within weeks of entering the Great War, the Ottoman Empire threw itself into three audacious offensives across three continents. Enver Pasha marched ninety thousand men into the frozen Caucasus mountains, dreaming of a new Tannenberg. Instead, his soldiers froze to death in mountain passes, and fewer than ten thousand stumbled back alive. On the Suez Canal, Ottoman forces crossed the Sinai Desert in total silence, only to be repulsed by Indian and Australian defenders. In Mesopotamia, British Indian forces captured Basra with deceptive ease, planting the seeds of an overconfidence that would prove fatal. But it was the Dardanelles that changed everything. Winston Churchill championed a bold naval assault to force the straits and knock the Ottomans out of the war. On March 18th, the Allied fleet charged through the narrows and ran straight into a minefield. Three battleships sank. The survivors limped away. Weeks later, thousands of Allied troops stormed ashore at Gallipoli, expecting a quick victory. Instead, they were met by an Ottoman defense of astonishing ferocity. At V Beach, soldiers were cut down by the hundreds before they could leave their boats. At Anzac Cove, Australians and New Zealanders clawed at cliffs under withering fire. By nightfall, the beaches were soaked in blood, and the Ottoman Empire had won its first real victory of the war.

Episode 3

Genocide, Gallipoli, and the Siege of Kut

As typhus ravages the east, the Ottoman government sets in motion the systematic annihilation of an entire people. Armenian men, women, and children are marched into the desert to die. At Gallipoli, Allied and Ottoman soldiers are locked in a nightmarish trench war where the dead rot between the lines. And in Mesopotamia, a reckless British advance ends in disaster as an entire army is trapped and besieged at Kut. Starvation, disease, and desperate rescue attempts follow. The walls are closing in, and surrender looms.

The year 1915 brought the war's darkest chapters. In eastern Anatolia, the catastrophe at Sarikamish gave Ottoman leaders the pretext they sought. Armenians were accused of treachery, and on April 24th, the intellectual and political leaders of the community were arrested in Istanbul. What followed was systematic extermination: deportation convoys marched into the Syrian desert, mass executions, children stolen from their families, survivors left to die of thirst and starvation on roads of bone. An Armenian priest named Grigoris Balakian resolved to survive and bear witness to what he saw. At Gallipoli, the campaign that was supposed to end the war devolved into a hellscape of trenches and disease. Soldiers lived among unburied corpses, fought hand-to-hand in tunnels, and endured artillery barrages that turned the peninsula into a moonscape. A daring August offensive at Sari Bair and Suvla Bay failed catastrophically when fresh divisions landed and simply sat on the beach. The eventual Allied evacuation was the campaign's only success. Meanwhile, in Mesopotamia, the British pushed recklessly toward Baghdad. At Ctesiphon, they met a force they could not defeat and retreated to the river town of Kut al-Amara. There, surrounded by Ottoman forces, an entire garrison of thirteen thousand men slowly starved as one relief attempt after another was beaten back. The siege was grinding toward a humiliation that would stain British military history for decades.

Episode 4

The Arab Revolt and the Turning Tide

A quiet conversation in Cairo plants the seed of a revolt that will reshape the Middle East. The Sharif of Mecca strikes a secret bargain with the British, and his sons lead Bedouin warriors against Ottoman garrisons. A young intelligence officer named Lawrence rides into the desert and emerges a legend. But behind the scenes, European diplomats are carving up the Arab lands in secret. As British forces finally break through at Gaza and Jerusalem falls, the promises made to the Arabs are already being betrayed.

While the Ottoman Empire bled at Gallipoli and Kut, a different kind of war was taking shape in the desert. Sharif Husayn of Mecca, guardian of Islam's holiest cities, entered into a fateful correspondence with the British High Commissioner in Egypt. In exchange for Arab independence, Husayn would raise a revolt against the Ottomans. But even as letters were exchanged, Britain and France were secretly negotiating the Sykes-Picot Agreement, dividing the Arab provinces between themselves. On the ground, the revolt began with the fall of Mecca and spread along the Red Sea coast. Faysal, Husayn's son, led Arab forces northward while a British intelligence officer, T.E. Lawrence, helped orchestrate daring raids on the Ottoman railway. The capture of Aqaba from the landward side stunned British planners and shifted the war's momentum. In Palestine, after two failed assaults on Gaza, General Allenby arrived with fresh troops and a new strategy. A brilliant deception at Beersheba broke through Ottoman lines, and Gaza fell at last. By December 1917, Allenby walked through the Jaffa Gate into Jerusalem, the first Christian conqueror since the Crusades. But the same month brought the Balfour Declaration, promising a Jewish homeland in Palestine. And in Russia, the Bolsheviks published the secret Sykes-Picot Agreement for all the world to see. The Arab Revolt had helped turn the tide of war, but the peace that followed would betray everything it fought for.

Episode 5

Collapse, Armistice, and the Postwar Middle East

Russia's collapse frees Ottoman divisions for a final offensive, but it is too late. Allenby launches a devastating cavalry charge at Megiddo that shatters the Ottoman line. Damascus falls as Arab and British forces race each other for the prize. The empire sues for peace, and an Allied fleet steams through the Dardanelles into Istanbul. But the armistice brings no peace. Broken promises ignite revolts from Egypt to Iraq, and the victors carve up the Middle East in ways whose consequences are still felt today.

The collapse of Imperial Russia in 1917 sent shockwaves across every front. The Bolsheviks published the secret Sykes-Picot Agreement, exposing Allied plans to partition the Arab lands. Enver Pasha seized the moment to launch an ambitious campaign into the Caucasus, dreaming of a pan-Turkic empire, while Allenby prepared a grand deception in Palestine. The Battle of Megiddo in September 1918 was one of the war's most decisive engagements. Allenby's cavalry shattered Ottoman lines and raced northward at astonishing speed. Damascus fell as Arab forces under Faysal and British troops converged on the city, each desperate to arrive first and claim the prize. The Ottoman government, its armies destroyed and its capital threatened, sued for peace. General Townshend, the man who had surrendered at Kut, was sent as an unlikely envoy to negotiate the Armistice of Moudros. An Allied fleet of forty-two vessels steamed through the cleared minefields of the Dardanelles and into Istanbul. But peace proved more dangerous than war. Egypt erupted in nationalist revolt. Iraq rose against British occupation. French troops expelled Faysal from Damascus. And in Anatolia, a Turkish officer named Mustafa Kemal rallied the remnants of the Ottoman army into a resistance movement that would forge a new nation from the wreckage. The borders drawn at Versailles and San Remo created countries that had never existed, divided peoples who had never been apart, and planted the seeds of conflicts that endure a century later.
