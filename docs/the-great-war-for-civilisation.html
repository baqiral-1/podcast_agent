The Great War for Civilisation

Nine episodes follow Robert Fisk’s reporting across Afghanistan, Iran-Iraq, Lebanon, Palestine, Algeria, the Gulf War, sanctions, 9/11, and the invasion of Iraq. It is a portrait of war’s human cost and the cycles that keep returning.

Episode 1

Afghanistan, Empire, and the First Encounter with Bin Laden

The Soviet war in Afghanistan seeds a jihad that will outlive its sponsors.

Robert Fisk enters Afghanistan as the Soviet war grinds on, a conflict where empires test their will on a brutal landscape. This episode moves from the refugee camps of Pakistan to the mountain front, following the mujahideen’s guerrilla war and the foreign money that keeps it alive. Stinger missiles and the CIA-ISI pipeline turn ideology into logistics. We meet the emerging Arab volunteers and a young Osama bin Laden, still treated as a useful ally rather than a global threat. The war is narrated through civilians caught between Soviet bombardment, warlord politics, and Western cameras hungry for a simple story. Fisk’s reporting complicates the narrative: the victors of one jihad may become the architects of the next. As the Soviets begin to withdraw, Pakistan and the United States declare success. The question that hangs over the last scenes is what happens when a war ends without a settlement. Peace will be deferred, not delivered.

Episode 2

Revolution and Fire: Iran, Iraq, and a War Without Limits

Revolution and invasion turn Iran and Iraq into a furnace of chemical war.

The Iranian revolution topples a king and electrifies the region, but the new Islamic Republic is immediately tested by war. The hostage crisis deepens the isolation of Tehran while emboldening its revolutionary rhetoric. This episode moves from Tehran’s upheaval to Saddam Hussein’s invasion, a conflict that devours a decade and a generation. Fisk reports from front lines where teenagers charge minefields and cities trade missile strikes. Chemical weapons appear, and the world looks away; oil politics matter more than law. Millions are displaced. The war redraws sectarian and nationalist identities, hardens dictatorship in Baghdad, and leaves Iran both proud and scarred. Western governments arm Iraq while condemning it in public, and the Gulf monarchies bankroll a stalemate that never feels stable. By the time the ceasefire arrives, no one has truly won. The question is what kind of Middle East this furnace has forged, and who will pay for it next.

Episode 3

Private Memory, Public Catastrophe

Lebanon’s civil war and the 1982 invasion expose a city’s wounds and a reporter’s memory.

Lebanon’s civil war turns a cosmopolitan country into a maze of checkpoints and disappearances. Fisk reports from Beirut as militias, foreign armies, and intelligence services carve the city into fear. This episode follows the Israeli invasion of 1982, the siege of West Beirut, and the massacres at Sabra and Shatila, where civilians are abandoned to their killers. The PLO’s expulsion and the collapse of the multinational force show how outsiders promise peace and then leave. It is also a story of memory: of journalists trying to bear witness, of families searching for the missing, of a war that never ends cleanly. We see how propaganda hardens every side and how truth becomes another casualty. The violence is intimate and bureaucratic at once, carried out by men who are neighbors one day and executioners the next. By the end, Lebanon is a warning: a country can survive on paper while its society is ripped apart.

Episode 4

Palestine: From Mandate Promises to Intifada Ruins

From mandate promises to intifada, Palestine lives under occupation and contested memory.

Palestine sits at the center of the region’s unresolved argument. Fisk traces the story from the British mandate and competing promises to the wars of 1948 and 1967, when displacement and occupation become permanent facts. The episode follows the growth of settlements, the daily humiliations of military rule, and the emergence of the PLO as both symbol and target. The exile in Lebanon, the 1982 invasion, and the grinding life under occupation sharpen the sense of irreversibility. The first intifada breaks the illusion that the conflict can be managed, and a generation of Palestinian youths takes to the streets with stones and strikes. Israeli politics hardens in response, even as secret negotiations hint at a possible peace. We see how language—terrorism, security, liberation—becomes another battlefield. By the end, the Oslo moment arrives with hope and deep suspicion. The episode asks whether any agreement can survive the realities it refuses to name.

Episode 5

Algeria's Dirty War

Algeria’s cancelled election ignites a dirty war of disappearances and massacres.

Algeria’s postcolonial story erupts in the 1990s when an election threatens military rule. The episode follows the rise of the Islamic Salvation Front, the army’s cancellation of the vote, and the descent into a dirty war that blurs every line between state and insurgent. Villages are massacred, disappearances become routine, and the security services operate in the shadows while claiming to defend the republic. Fisk visits survivors who describe night raids and mass graves, and he questions the official story that blames only the Islamists. Fisk reports on the fear that grips ordinary people who no longer know who is killing whom. The conflict is sold abroad as a fight against extremism, yet it devours civilians first. By the time the violence begins to burn out, the country is exhausted and its political life hollowed. The silence afterward feels uneasy. The episode asks whether stability bought through terror can ever produce legitimacy.

Episode 6

Desert Storm and the Betrayed Aftermath

Desert Storm’s swift victory leaves uprisings crushed and sanctions entrenched.

Saddam’s invasion of Kuwait triggers a U.S.-led war that seems to promise a quick, clean victory. Fisk reports on the air campaign’s destruction, the highway of death, and the language of precision that hides civilian cost. Scud strikes on Israel, coalition diplomacy, and UN sanctions become the new architecture of containment. It is fragile. The ceasefire is a pause, not peace. The episode follows the uprising that follows the ceasefire: Kurds in the north and Shiites in the south rise up, believing the coalition will back them. Instead, Saddam’s forces crush the revolts while the world watches. The result is a regime left in power, a population left exposed, and a region that learns the price of American promises. The war ends without settlement, and Iraq becomes a sealed box of sanctions, smuggling, and suffering. Fisk’s reporting asks whether the conflict truly ended in 1991, or simply laid the ground for a much larger reckoning.

Episode 7

Slow Violence: Sanctions, Toxic Legacies, and the Arms Bazaar

Sanctions, toxic legacies, and the arms trade reveal a slower, bureaucratic violence.

After the Gulf War, Iraq is strangled by sanctions and a sanctions-busting economy. Fisk reports on hospitals without medicine, children’s malnutrition, and the quiet arithmetic of death that never makes front pages. The episode explores the politics of embargo: how Washington and London insist the pressure is humane while the regime adapts and civilians pay. The Oil-for-Food program becomes a symbol of corruption, with the UN caught between relief and complicity. We also enter the arms bazaar—dealers, governments, and covert networks that profit from perpetual conflict, from the Gulf to the Balkans. The toxic legacy of war lingers in contaminated water and unexploded ordnance, turning peace into another hazard. This is the violence of bureaucracy and economics, slower but no less deadly. Few admit the cost. By the end, Iraq is weakened but not transformed, and the question is whether the world has created a catastrophe it can no longer walk away from.

Episode 8

Levantine Ghosts, 9/11, and the Road to Iraq

9/11 recasts the region and pushes the United States toward Iraq.

The late 1990s feel like a long warning. Fisk moves through the Levant as old wars leave new ghosts—Hizbullah’s rise, Syria’s grip on Lebanon, and the unfinished business of Palestine. Then 9/11 arrives, and the region is recast as the theater of a global war. Meanwhile, Iraq remains under sanctions, and Saudi bases and Israeli-Palestinian violence fuel radical recruitment. The region hears drums of war. It is not yet unstoppable. This episode tracks how the attacks are interpreted in the Middle East and in Washington, and how the hunt for al‑Qaeda morphs into an argument for invading Iraq. Intelligence is stretched, history is simplified, and dissent is dismissed as disloyalty. Fisk reports from Afghanistan and the Gulf as the rhetoric of liberation grows louder than the evidence. The episode ends on the eve of invasion, when the logic of preemption has become policy and the last chances to stop it are slipping away.

Episode 9

The Sack of Baghdad and the Age of Occupation

The fall of Baghdad opens an occupation defined by looting, insurgency, and sectarian war.

Baghdad falls quickly, but the real war begins afterward. Fisk reports from a city stripped of order: ministries looted, museums emptied, and ordinary Iraqis left to improvise survival under occupation. The episode follows the dissolution of the Iraqi army, the vacuum it creates, and the rapid rise of insurgent and sectarian violence. De‑Baathification and the disbanded police force turn resistance into a mass movement, and Fallujah becomes a symbol. Checkpoints become targets, militias take neighborhoods, and the promise of liberation curdles into humiliation. Abu Ghraib’s images crystallize the collapse of moral authority, while U.S. officials insist a new Iraq is being built. Fisk listens to families, doctors, and shopkeepers who measure policy in injuries and funerals. By the time elections arrive, the country is fractured and the occupation’s legitimacy is gone. The episode ends with a question that echoes back to Afghanistan: what does victory mean when the state itself is broken?
