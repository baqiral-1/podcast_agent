The Great War for Civilisation

A ten-episode narrative history drawn from Robert Fisk's eyewitness chronicle of the modern Middle East, tracing imperial collapse, intervention, occupation, and the human wreckage left behind.

Episode 1

Afghanistan Before the Reckoning

A 1997 journey to bin Laden’s mountain redoubt opens into the Soviet war and the jihad that followed.

Fisk’s 1997 journey to an interview with Osama bin Laden opens the episode, then the story rewinds to the Soviet invasion and the brutal decade that followed. We trace how Afghan resistance was armed and financed, how Pakistan and Saudi Arabia became conduits, and how foreign myths about empire shaped the war. Civilians describe bombing, starvation, and the collapse of Kabul’s ordinary life, while warlords and clerics recast the conflict as a holy struggle. The episode connects earlier British interventions to the late-Cold War jihad and to the Taliban’s ascent, showing how violence was normalized and memory weaponized. By the time Fisk leaves through the Khyber Pass, the road itself feels like a witness — to an unfinished war that was already pointing toward the catastrophes of the next decade.

Episode 2

Iran: Coup, Revolution, and the Road to War

From Mossadeq’s overthrow to Saddam’s gamble, the region slides toward the Iran-Iraq War.

Beginning with Saddam’s 1980 nuclear denials, the episode unspools the history that made war with Iran seem inevitable. It moves from the 1953 overthrow of Mossadeq and the Shah’s authoritarian modernization to the 1979 revolution and hostage crisis, then to Saddam’s gamble that Iran was weak. The story captures how early offensives stalled into attrition and trench warfare. Fisk follows the first years of the Iran-Iraq War: the river battles, the tanker war, and the global courtship of Baghdad even as poison gas and repression mount. Western navies enter the Gulf, oil and security blur, and missile strikes like the USS Stark expose the danger of “neutral” protection. The episode ends in the language of martyrdom that sustained the conflict — a war prolonged by fear, faith, and international bargains.

Episode 3

The Iran-Iraq Inferno

Civilian airliners fall, trenches grind on, and the war’s violence echoes older regional tragedies.

Episode three opens with the wreckage of Iran Air 655, a civilian airliner shot down over the Gulf, and follows the war’s grind: trenches, mass assaults, chemical attacks, and brutal fighting around Basra and in Kurdistan. Fisk reports from both sides, contrasting Iranian narratives of sacrifice with Iraqi memories of exhaustion and terror. The conflict widens into a market: arms shipments, secrecy, and diplomatic cover that keep the killing going. He then steps back to older layers of violence, including the Armenian genocide and its survivors, to show how the region’s wars echo across generations. The episode is less about strategy than about bodies and memory — a portrait of a war that consumed civilians and left behind competing histories of grief.

Episode 4

Palestine: Betrayal, Leadership, and the Colonial Continuum

From the mandate’s double promises to Oslo’s stalemate, the Palestinian story becomes a battle over memory.

Palestine is told through contested memory: Haj Amin al-Husseini’s grave, the British mandate’s double promises, and the long arc from 1948’s catastrophe to exile in Lebanon. Fisk traces the rise of the PLO, Arafat’s leadership, the Beirut sieges, and the compromises of Oslo, while settlements and checkpoints expand. The episode moves through the First Intifada, the birth of the Palestinian Authority, and security courts that police dissent as well as occupation. Refugee camps in Lebanon and Gaza become permanent cities, while settlements and bypass roads carve the land into fragments. Civilians live inside curfews, closures, and the politics of documentation, while history itself becomes a battlefield. The story insists on continuity: dispossession, resistance, and the fight over who is allowed to narrate the past.

Episode 5

Palestine Under Occupation and Algeria's Mirror

Khan Younis in 2001 opens into the Second Intifada, then Algeria’s civil war becomes a brutal parallel.

Beginning in the ruins of Khan Younis in 2001, the episode follows life under occupation: house demolitions, targeted killings, and the political calculus of Hamas leaders like Abdel Aziz Rantissi. Civilians navigate curfews, checkpoints, and the fear of raids as the Second Intifada intensifies. Fisk then turns to Algeria’s civil war to show how state violence and insurgent terror blur into one another. In Blida, in the triangle of death, massacres and disappearances hollow out villages while security forces and militias trade accusations. The testimony of Lt. Habib Souaïdia, who breaks the code of silence, brings names and dates back into view. Algeria becomes a mirror that shows how terror and counterterror feed each other. The through-line is the same: the dead, and the refusal of euphemism to make them disappear.

Episode 6

After the Gulf War: The Abandoned Uprising

Western calls to rebel are followed by mass graves, crushed uprisings, and a war sold as clean.

After the Gulf War, radio broadcasts and Western leaders urge Iraqis to rise against Saddam. Basra answers, and the Shia and Kurdish uprisings spread before being crushed while coalition forces stand aside. The crushed rebellion leaves towns scarred and the promise of liberation hollow. Fisk follows the consequences: mass graves, devastated cities, and the long abandonment of those who believed the promises. He also traces the war’s aftermath beyond Iraq — the treatment of Palestinians in Kuwait, the media pool system that sanitized combat, and the dependence of reporters on military access. The episode ends in the southern marshes, where Saddam’s regime drains the wetlands to punish the Marsh Arabs and erase a way of life. The “clean war” becomes a landscape of betrayal, euphemism, and slow violence.

Episode 7

Desert Triumph, Desert Crime

The Gulf War’s victory narrative masks mass graves, sanctions, and a weapons economy that keeps killing.

Mutla Ridge and the Highway of Death frame the aftermath of 1991: a victory narrated as clean, a battlefield full of uncounted bodies. The episode tracks sanctions, inspections, and the long argument over weapons of mass destruction as Iraq is bombed and starved in the name of containment. Fisk follows the arms economy that feeds both war and peace — from Western sales to the moral language that rationalizes them — and the way media choreography turns graves into photo opportunities. The story widens to Lebanon and Palestine, where missiles and targeted killings show how weapons travel. By the end, war has become a permanent condition: sanctions as siege, bombing as routine, and memory as the only ledger for the dead.

Episode 8

Kings, Ruins, and the Long Road to 9/11

9/11 becomes a hinge into Lebanon’s wars, Arab nationalism, and the next round of “liberations.”

On the morning of 9/11, Fisk is still writing about Sabra and Chatila, and the episode uses that hinge to move backward through Lebanon’s wars and the longer history of Arab nationalism. We revisit the 1916–18 martyrs of Beirut, the fractures of mandate rule, and the cycles of assassination and bombardment that followed. The story passes through the U.S. bombing of Beirut, the 1982 massacre, and the deaths of civilians such as Raafat, whose home was erased in 1986. The assassination of Ahmed Shah Massoud and the coming Afghanistan war cast a shadow over every scene. Afghanistan and Iraq hover ahead as the next “liberations.” The episode closes with a question about repetition: whether the region’s ruins are warnings or rehearsals for the wars still coming.

Episode 9

The Case for War

The statue falls in Fardus Square, but looting, casualties, and insurgency arrive with liberation.

Fardus Square’s statue-toppling looks like liberation, but the same day brings looting, hospital chaos, and a new question: what do the Americans want? Fisk follows the first months of occupation as ministries are stripped, security collapses, and the first suicide attacks erupt. Civilians die in markets and on highways, while officials rename the violence as “collateral” and move on. The narrative pulls in older ghosts — British graves from 1921, the abandoned Shia of 1991, the poison gas of Halabja — to show that 2003 is a return, not a reset. The war of liberation ends as resistance begins, and figures like Ali Jaffar al-Nomani signal the insurgency to come. By the end, liberation has flipped into resistance, and the war has found its next form.

Episode 10

Baghdad Falls, the Occupation Begins

From Highway 8 to Fallujah, the fight over evidence and narrative becomes part of the war itself.

An explosion in Beirut in 2005 prompts a question — was that Baghdad? — and the episode follows the widening arc of occupation violence from Iraq back into the region. We move along Highway 8, through Fallujah, kidnappings, and the market for execution videos, where images become instructions. Fisk digs into the struggle over evidence: disputed bullet holes, suppressed reports, and the fight to name the dead. Bin Laden tapes, staged briefings, and competing narratives blur what the public is allowed to see. The story ranges from Abu Ghraib to the second intifada’s contested footage, showing how language and narrative are weaponized alongside guns. What endures are documents, testimony, and graves — a stubborn archive that outlasts propaganda and insists the dead be counted.
