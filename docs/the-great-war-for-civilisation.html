The Great War for Civilisation

An eight-episode narrative history drawn from Robert Fisk's eyewitness chronicle of the modern Middle East, tracing imperial collapse, intervention, occupation, and the human wreckage left behind.

Episode 1

Empire, Insurgency, and the Road to Catastrophe

We open with the fault lines that shape the modern Middle East: imperial ambition, Afghan resistance, and the collapse of easy colonial myths.

Moving from the Soviet war in Afghanistan through the rise of armed movements and the politics of intervention, this episode follows the region as outside powers learn the wrong lessons from violence they only partly understand. Fisk's reporting keeps the story close to the ground, where grand strategy repeatedly breaks against memory, terrain, and rage. By the end, the path to a wider regional war is already visible: great powers are committed, local societies are destabilized, and the language of liberation has begun to mask a much darker future.

Episode 2

Desert Storm and the Manufacture of Victory

This episode follows the rush from crisis to war as Iraq's invasion of Kuwait becomes a proving ground for a new kind of televised conflict.

We trace the buildup to the Gulf War, the choreography of alliance politics, the language of precision and inevitability, and the brutal human cost concealed behind images of clean military success. Fisk stays with the civilians, conscripts, and wrecked landscapes that triumphal narratives prefer to skip. The story is not only about battlefield victory, but about how modern war is narrated, sold, and remembered. What looks like resolution in 1991 leaves deeper grievances behind, setting the stage for future occupations, sanctions, and permanent instability.

Episode 3

From Ruin to Reckoning in the Arab World

As the aftermath of war spreads, this episode moves through massacre, repression, exile, and the long memory of abandoned promises.

Fisk's reporting connects state violence, colonial residue, and regional humiliation into a broader story about how the Middle East was pushed toward repeated crisis. We move across the politics of atrocity, the burden placed on refugees and survivors, and the way official histories erase the people forced to live through their consequences. The episode also widens geographically, showing how the same habits of denial and intervention travel from one conflict to the next. By the close, the region is not stabilizing but hardening, with every settlement already carrying the seeds of another war.

Episode 4

Sanctions, Airstrikes, and the New Permanence of War

This episode charts the grim years when war no longer arrives as a single event but settles into everyday life through sanctions, bombing campaigns, and open-ended coercion.

Fisk follows the logic of punishment as it moves from military doctrine into hospitals, infrastructure, and ordinary homes, exposing the gap between humanitarian rhetoric and lived reality. We examine how international legitimacy is claimed, how moral language is weaponized, and how suffering becomes background noise for distant audiences. The Gulf War's aftermath turns into a system rather than an exception. What emerges is a portrait of a region held in suspension: formally managed from abroad, steadily broken from within, and pushed toward still more explosive confrontations.

Episode 5

Graves, Plague, and the Machinery of Endless Conflict

This episode uses the rerun covering Chapters 17 through 20 and should be published as podcast episode 5.

Fisk moves through landscapes filled with death, disease, profiteering, and exhausted political illusions, tracing how war degrades not only cities and institutions but the moral imagination of everyone involved. We follow the trade in arms, the spread of fear, the bureaucracies that normalize mass suffering, and the stubborn political habits that keep drawing rulers back to violence. The story is cumulative rather than episodic: each campaign inherits the lies of the last one. By the end, war has become both industry and ritual, sustained by memory, money, and leaders who keep mistaking devastation for order.

Episode 6

Israel, Palestine, and a Conflict Without End

Here the series narrows its focus to the Israeli-Palestinian conflict, but the larger themes of empire, memory, and impunity remain central.

Fisk traces the accumulation of grievances across occupation, uprising, retaliation, and failed diplomacy, refusing the false symmetry that often obscures who bears the weight of daily violence. The episode stays close to the lived consequences of checkpoints, settlement expansion, military force, and political theater, showing how each promised breakthrough deepens disillusionment. This is also a story about language: security, terrorism, peace, and history itself are all contested terrain. What emerges is not a frozen conflict, but an active system of domination and resistance that shapes the region's political conscience.

Episode 7

9/11 and the Architecture of the War on Terror

This episode follows the shock of September 11 and the global response that quickly transformed grief into doctrine.

Fisk places the attacks inside a much longer history of intervention, resentment, and strategic blindness, challenging the idea that the new war began from nowhere. We trace the political uses of fear, the expansion of military power, and the speed with which complexity is flattened into slogans about civilization and barbarism. The result is a world newly organized around permanent emergency. Yet Fisk's reporting insists on continuity: the war on terror did not replace older conflicts, it absorbed them. The episode captures the moment when exception became policy and retaliation became a governing principle.

Episode 8

Into the Wilderness: Witness, Memory, and the Meaning of War

The final episode closes the series in a reflective register without losing sight of the bodies, ruins, and betrayals that fill the book.

Fisk returns to the question that runs underneath every chapter: what does war do to truth, to memory, and to the people who are asked to survive its aftermath? The episode gathers the long arc of the series into a meditation on reporting, denial, accountability, and the dangerous comfort of official narratives. It is about the persistence of civilians, the failures of power, and the historian's duty to resist simplification. The end is not consoling. It is a reckoning with how modern states wage war and how civilization repeatedly excuses it.
