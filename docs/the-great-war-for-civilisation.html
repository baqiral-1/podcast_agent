The Great War for Civilisation

A journalist spends three decades in the most dangerous places on earth, bearing witness to the wars, revolutions, and betrayals that define the modern Middle East. From a mountain tent with Osama bin Laden to the trenches of the Iran-Iraq War, from besieged Beirut to the smoking ruins of Baghdad, every chapter brings another reckoning with violence, complicity, and the human cost of empire. Governments lie, alliances shift, and the dead pile up while the powerful look away. This is the story of a century of conflict told by the man who was there when the bombs fell.

Episode 1

Into the Lion's Den: Meeting bin Laden

A cryptic phone call in Beirut. A flight into Taliban-controlled Afghanistan on an airline with no safety record. A drive through opium country, and then a midnight summons into the mountains. Three times a journalist sits face to face with the most wanted man in the world, and each meeting reveals a darker vision. In Sudan, bin Laden cleans his teeth and speaks softly. In the Afghan mountains, he issues warnings no one will heed. The final meeting is lit by lamplight, and the words spoken there will echo across history.

In December 1993, a journalist traveled to Sudan and sat across from a tall, soft-spoken Saudi exile named Osama bin Laden. The man was wealthy, connected, and building something. Their conversation was polite, almost gentle. Three years later, after a cryptic phone call to Beirut, the same journalist boarded an Ariana Afghan Airlines flight with no in-flight service, no safety card, and almost no functioning instruments, and landed in Jalalabad. He waited for days in a hotel of manicured lawns surrounded by an opium-soaked province. Then came the midnight knock: car keys rapping against a bedroom window. A convoy into the mountains. Armed men on every ridge. In a military tent staked into the earth, bin Laden sat on stained mattresses and delivered a message the world would not understand until it was far too late. He spoke of Saudi Arabia, of American bases on holy soil, of a coming reckoning. He invoked the metaphor of a single kilogram of TNT detonating in a country that had never heard an explosion. His sons sat beside him for a photograph by lamplight. On the drive back to Jalalabad, a great light appeared in the northern mountains, and the journalist could not tell if it was a missile test or something far stranger. A third meeting followed, higher in the mountains, surrounded by Arab fighters with Kalashnikovs. The warnings grew sharper. The world was not listening.

The Soviet Graveyard: Reporting the Afghan War

On Christmas Eve 1979, Soviet tanks rolled into Afghanistan, and a journalist raced to witness the occupation firsthand. Riding in freezing military convoys under ambush, dodging secret police in Kabul hotel rooms, and smuggling dispatches across the border by bus, he documented a war the world barely noticed. Afghan villages burned. Schools were destroyed. Night letters promised death. And on a terrifying ride with Convoy 58, bullets cracked off the highway as the Red Army discovered it could not win the war it started.

The story begins not in Afghanistan but with a book: a mother's gift to her son before the First World War, inscribed with an imperial confidence that would not survive the century. When the Soviet Union invaded Afghanistan on Christmas Eve 1979, a journalist arrived two weeks later to find armored columns still pouring across the Amu Darya. What followed was years of reporting from inside a war zone that most Western journalists could not reach. In Jalalabad, nighttime explosions shook the darkness. In Kabul, the secret police visited the journalist's driver and threatened consequences. Schools were burned by mujahideen who accused them of teaching communism. Night letters appeared on doors promising retribution. The journalist developed an ingenious system with a local bus driver named Ali, smuggling dispatches across the Pakistani border while Soviet checkpoints multiplied. Then came the most dangerous assignment: riding with Convoy 58, a column of Soviet tanks and trucks grinding through the Salang Pass at two degrees below zero. When the ambush came, it came without warning: explosions, gunfire, burning vehicles. Afghan villagers in orange shawls watched from the roadside as the convoy fought through. Back in Kabul, Western journalists were being expelled one by one. The doors were closing. On one of his last trips, the journalist found himself under fire at Sorkh Rud, sheltering in an Afghan family's home while artillery shook the walls. The Soviet war was already lost. The question was how many would die before anyone admitted it.

Episode 3

Revolution and Hostages: Iran's Transformation

In snowy Oxford, a retired spy reveals how Western intelligence overthrew Iran's democracy decades ago. The consequences arrive in 1979 as revolution sweeps Tehran. Firing squads execute the old regime's men at dawn. A hanging judge with twinkling eyes dispenses death sentences between bites of ice cream. Students storm the American embassy and begin reassembling shredded CIA documents with the patience of carpet weavers. And in a small village, a mullah sits among his people, explaining why the revolution has come to Kahak.

The roots of Iran's revolution reach back to a snowy day in Oxford, where a seventy-nine-year-old former British intelligence officer quietly confirmed how he and the CIA orchestrated the 1953 coup that destroyed Iranian democracy. The Shah they restored to power built a regime on terror: his secret police, SAVAK, tortured dissidents in underground chambers, while the monarch staged extravagant pageants celebrating twenty-five centuries of Persian kingship. When revolution came, it came with a fury proportional to the repression. Ayatollah Khomeini returned from exile, and within weeks firing squads were executing the old regime's officials on Tehran rooftops. Prime Minister Hoveyda was shot after a mockery of a trial. A former warrant officer was condemned by revolutionary courts in Qom. And then arrived Khalkhali, the hanging judge: a small, plump man who dispensed death sentences with terrifying cheerfulness, brandishing a miniature pink plastic spoon while eating ice cream between verdicts. At the American embassy, students held fifty-two hostages while painstakingly reassembling shredded CIA documents, revealing decades of American interference. The journalist wandered revolutionary Iran by train on a student rail card, witnessing a middle-class revolution beginning to devour its own. He gained an audience with Khomeini in Qom, watched the theocracy harden around the hostage crisis, and traveled to the village of Kahak, where a mullah explained what the revolution meant to people who had nothing. Iran had been transformed, and the consequences would consume the region for decades.

Episode 4

Saddam's Kingdom: Iraq Before the Storm

A proclamation peeled from a wall in 1917 promised the people of Baghdad liberation. The British lied then, and the pattern never stopped. Decades of coups, betrayals, and aerial bombardment gave way to a regime built on suspicion, torture, and cult worship. A nuclear scientist endured twenty-two days of continuous torture for refusing to build a bomb. Dissidents were murdered in London. Shia scholars were silenced with bullets. And the West looked the other way, because Saddam was useful. But a storm was gathering on the Iranian border.

The story of modern Iraq begins with a lie. In March 1917, a British soldier copied out a proclamation to the people of Baghdad: 'Our armies do not come as conquerors but as liberators.' The reality was aerial bombardment, poison gas, and colonial exploitation. Iraq was stitched together from three Ottoman provinces whose peoples had little in common. When the British mandate ended, it left behind a fragile monarchy, a fractured society, and a military that would stage coup after coup. By the time Saddam Hussein seized absolute power, Iraq had become a republic of fear. The regime's methods were revealed through harrowing testimony. Dr. Hussain Shahristani, a nuclear chemist, was tortured for twenty-two days because he refused to work on an atomic bomb. Agents murdered dissidents in London hotels. The Shia majority was crushed, with Ayatollah Bakr Sadr executed and his sister reportedly tortured to death. Mass graves filled with Kurds, communists, and anyone who drew suspicion. And yet Western governments continued to arm and support the regime. Britain understood Iraq's brutality and chose complicity. The journalist Farzad Bazoft was hanged despite international pleas, and arms deals continued uninterrupted. Meanwhile, exiled opposition figures fought each other as much as they fought Baghdad, and the tangled web of Iraqi-Iranian enmity was drawing both nations toward open conflict. By 1980, oppression lay like a blanket over the entire region, and the drift toward a catastrophic war was accelerating.

Episode 5

The Trenches of Armageddon: The Iran-Iraq War

It opens with a poem about poison gas and ends at the gates of a cemetery where tens of thousands lie buried. Between those bookends: eight years of industrialized slaughter. Boy soldiers march into minefields wearing keys to paradise around their necks. Chemical weapons melt the lungs of Iranian troops while the world sells arms to both sides. A passenger jet is shot from the sky by an American warship. And in prison cells behind the lines, a different kind of killing is happening. This is the war that never truly ended.

In September 1980, Iraq invaded Iran, and what followed was the longest conventional war of the twentieth century. Eight years. A million dead. Trenches stretching for hundreds of miles. Chemical weapons deployed with industrial efficiency. The journalist watched through Iraqi army binoculars as commandos crossed the Karun River toward Khorramshahr, Iran's Stalingrad. He recorded dispatches under artillery fire that never reached their destination. In the Howeiza marshes, an Iraqi commander devised a method of annihilation using fire, electricity, and poison. Iran sent child soldiers into minefields, each wearing a small key around his neck, the promised key to paradise. At Friday prayers in Tehran, thousands of worshippers watched young volunteers prepare for martyrdom. Iraq used mustard gas, tabun, and sarin, while American satellite intelligence helped direct the attacks. The world sold weapons to both sides. Israel sold arms to Iran. France sold jets to Iraq. American Hellfire missiles and Chinese silkworms flowed into the region. When the USS Vincennes shot down Iran Air Flight 655, killing 290 civilians, the American government blamed the pilot and decorated the ship's captain. Inside Iran's prisons, political prisoners were being executed by the thousands in a secret campaign of extermination. When the guns finally fell silent, the ruins of Khorramshahr stood as a monument to futility. South of Tehran, at the vast cemetery of Behesht-i-Zahra, tens of thousands of graves stretched to the horizon, each one marking a life consumed by a war that achieved nothing.

My Father's War: WWI, Memory, and the Armenian Genocide

A father's war stories lead a son to the battlefields of France, where a tin box of medals hides a terrible secret. A soldier was executed at dawn, and the journalist's father may have witnessed it. But the trail from the Western Front leads far beyond France, to a hillside in the Syrian desert littered with bones from 1915. In a Beirut home for the blind, the last survivors of the Armenian genocide whisper their memories. And across the world, an organized campaign works to erase what happened from history.

This is a story that begins on a father's knee and ends in the killing fields of three continents. When Bill Fisk sat his son down and showed him his Great War medals, he planted an obsession that would take decades to unravel. The journalist retraced his father's footsteps through the shattered towns of northern France, finding bullet holes still fresh in church walls and graves of executed soldiers buried shamefully apart from their comrades. The story of Gunner Frank Wills, who deserted in Paris and was shot at dawn, raised haunting questions about what Bill Fisk had witnessed and what he had chosen to forget. But history's fingers never relax their grip. From the Western Front, the trail led to the hill of Margada in the eastern Syrian desert, where bones from 1915 lay scattered among volcanic stones. Armenian deportees had been marched to this place and left to die. In Beirut, at the Armenian home for the blind, the last survivors spoke of horrors witnessed as children: Zakar Berberian, eighty-nine years old, still carrying the memories of the death marches. In Smyrna, survivors of the Armenian genocide walked straight into fresh horrors as the city burned. The journalist traced the architecture of denial across continents: Turkish government campaigns, compliant academics, newspapers that softened the word 'genocide' to 'tragedy.' Hitler himself had asked, 'Who now remembers the Armenians?' The answer, as this episode reveals, is that some people never stopped remembering, even when the world demanded they forget.

The Promised Land: Israel-Palestine from Balfour to Oslo

It begins at a grave in Beirut and ends with a handshake on the White House lawn that promised everything and delivered nothing. Between them: the Balfour Declaration, the massacre at Deir Yassin, seven hundred fifty thousand refugees, and decades of war, occupation, and broken promises. A Holocaust survivor lives yards from where Palestinian families were slaughtered. Settlements multiply while diplomats celebrate peace. And in Gaza, a pharmacist says his prayers and walks toward a bus carrying a bomb.

In a corner of Beirut's Palestinian Martyrs' Cemetery, among the graves of guerrillas killed during Israel's 1982 invasion, the ghost of the Grand Mufti haunts the story of a conflict that has consumed generations. The Balfour Declaration of 1917 promised a Jewish homeland in a land already inhabited by hundreds of thousands of Palestinians. What followed was a cascade of catastrophes. In April 1948, the massacre at Deir Yassin sent shockwaves through the Arab world and triggered a mass exodus. Josef Kleinman, a Holocaust survivor from Dachau, settled in a Jerusalem suburb built on the ruins of that very village. Seven hundred fifty thousand Palestinians became refugees, and their descendants now number in the millions. The decades that followed brought war after war: 1948, 1967, 1973. Yasser Arafat built a revolution in exile, surviving assassination attempts, political isolation, and his own catastrophic decision to support Saddam Hussein's invasion of Kuwait. The Madrid peace conference of 1991 was a theater of accusation. Oslo, hatched in secret, produced the famous handshake between Arafat and Rabin on the White House lawn, but the agreement was fatally flawed from the start. It deferred every hard question: Jerusalem, refugees, borders, settlements. Meanwhile, Israeli settlement construction accelerated throughout the Oslo years, doubling the settler population even as diplomats celebrated peace. Rabin was assassinated by an Israeli extremist. Arafat returned to Gaza to find a trap waiting for him. And in December 1993, three months after the handshake, the first suicide bombing signaled that the forces opposed to peace were already winning.

Blood and Destruction: The Second Intifada

Sharon marches to the holy places, and the Second Intifada erupts in blood. Refugee camps are bulldozed. Children are shot at checkpoints. Suicide bombers walk into pizza restaurants. And inside the besieged cities of the West Bank, a journalist documents what occupation really looks like: demolished homes, erased identities, and a population trapped between an army with tanks and militants with explosives. In Jenin, the battle for a refugee camp becomes a fight over a single word. No one emerges clean from what follows.

In September 2000, Ariel Sharon marched to the Muslim holy places above the Temple Mount, accompanied by a thousand Israeli police. What followed was an explosion of violence that shattered whatever remained of the Oslo peace. At Khan Younis refugee camp on Easter Sunday morning, the journalist stood amid rubble that looked like an earthquake's aftermath. At Ayosha Junction in Ramallah, the daily ritual of stone-throwing and live fire had become a grim routine. Israeli soldiers described it as theater. Palestinians described it as resistance. Then came the suicide bombings: the Sbarro pizzeria in Jerusalem, the Wall Street Cafe, buses and markets across Israel. Mobile phones rang on the belts of the dead. In the aftermath, a question lingered that no headline could answer: what goes through the mind of a person in those final seconds? Operation Defensive Shield brought Israeli tanks into every major Palestinian city. In Jenin refugee camp, Palestinian fighters engaged in the fiercest urban combat of the intifada. The battle left a swath of destruction that triggered a bitter international debate about whether the word 'massacre' applied. In Bethlehem, fighters barricaded themselves inside the Church of the Nativity while snipers took positions on the rooftops. In Gaza, children were killed near settlements, their fathers left to wonder what had driven them toward the wire. The machinery of occupation ground on: bulldozed homes, uprooted olive groves, checkpoints where humiliation was routine. And through it all, the language of the conflict itself became a battleground.

Algeria's Dirty War

A cemetery in Algiers reads: 'Me today, you tomorrow.' When the government steals an election to prevent an Islamist victory, it ignites a civil war of unspeakable savagery. Throat-cuttings, car bombs, baby-strangling. Monks are kidnapped from a mountain monastery. Entire villages are massacred while the army watches from nearby barracks. The government sends delegations to learn torture techniques. And journalists who investigate too closely find themselves in the triangle of death. Two hundred thousand dead, and the world barely noticed.

Above the iron gates of the St. Eugene cemetery in Algiers, an inscription reads: 'Me today, you tomorrow.' It is a fitting epitaph for a country that consumed itself in one of the most brutal civil wars of the late twentieth century. In January 1992, the Algerian government cancelled elections that the Islamic Salvation Front was about to win. What followed was a descent into savagery that defied comprehension. Armed groups carried out throat-cuttings, beheadings, and village massacres on a medieval scale. The government responded with torture, disappearances, and extrajudicial killing. In the town of Blida, eight thousand mourners gathered for the funeral of a murdered sheikh. Journalists developed grim routines for survival, driving through the triangle of death where colleagues had been murdered. The government circulated dossiers of postmortem photographs as a form of psychological warfare. In the high mountains, gunmen seized seven French monks from the monastery of Tibherine, men of peace who had offered medical care to villagers. In 1997, the massacres at Rais and Bentalha killed hundreds in single nights, with survivors reporting that soldiers stationed nearby did nothing. A woman named Dalilah testified about torture inside the security apparatus: elderly prisoners beaten, suspects electrocuted, bodies dumped. Former security personnel came forward to describe what they had witnessed. The international community's response was tepid at best. By 2002, up to two hundred thousand Algerians had been killed. The dirty war exposed a truth about modern conflict: when both sides commit atrocities, the victims are the ones who disappear.

Desert Storm: The 1991 Gulf War

A journalist curls up in the crew seat of a 707, flying east toward a war that will reshape the Middle East. In Saudi Arabia, generals dodge questions about oil while a hotel ballroom becomes the nerve center of destruction. French journalists sneak past checkpoints into a ghost town. The ground war lasts a hundred hours, but the highway of death lasts forever. And when Iraqis rise up against Saddam, heeding an American call to revolt, they discover that the liberation promised on the radio was never meant for them.

In August 1990, Saddam Hussein's army stormed into Kuwait, and within hours the machinery of a massive Western military intervention was set in motion. King Fahd invited American forces onto Saudi soil, a decision that would radicalize a generation. In Dhahran, a grotesquely decorated hotel ballroom became the Joint Information Bureau, where military briefers delivered sanitized accounts of the air campaign to hundreds of journalists corralled into press pools. General Schwarzkopf deflected questions about oil. French journalists, with characteristic defiance, broke free and drove into the desert alone. The air war lasted weeks. The Amariya bunker bombing killed hundreds of civilians, and the military called it a command center. Daisy cutter bombs shook the desert floor. When the ground war finally came, it lasted a hundred hours. Kuwait City emerged scarred beyond recognition, its sky black with the smoke of burning oil wells. North of the city, the highway of death presented a scene of apocalyptic horror: the body of an Iraqi general half out of a stolen limousine, soldiers incinerated in their vehicles, the retreating army annihilated from the air. But the true betrayal came afterward. A CIA-run radio station had called on Iraqis to rise against Saddam. When Shia communities in the south and Kurds in the north answered that call, American forces stood by and watched as Saddam's Republican Guard crushed the uprising. Mass graves would not be discovered for twelve years. The coalition had liberated Kuwait and abandoned Iraq to its tormentors.

Sanctions, Sickness, and the Merchants of Death

A tattered bouquet of flowers on Mutla Ridge marks the anniversary of invasion. Below, a new kind of weapon is killing Iraqis: sanctions that strangle hospitals, starve children, and reduce a country to selling its last possessions. In Basra, a doctor maps a cancer epidemic among children who live near battlefields contaminated by depleted uranium. And at the Abu Dhabi arms fair, merchants sell the very weapons that caused the suffering, their moral arithmetic as clean as their tailored suits. The trail of a single missile fragment leads to a factory hidden behind manicured lawns.

One year after the Gulf War, a tattered bouquet of flowers on Mutla Ridge marked the spot where thousands of Iraqi soldiers had been incinerated on the highway of death. But the killing had not stopped. United Nations sanctions, maintained for nearly thirteen years, became a weapon more deadly than missiles. Hospitals ran out of medicine. Children died of treatable diseases. A country that had once been among the most developed in the Arab world was reduced to selling its last possessions on the street. In Basra, Dr. Jawad al-Ali mapped cancer clusters among children living near battlefields where Allied forces had used depleted uranium ammunition. The wards were filled with children suffering from leukemia and lymphomas at rates that defied medical explanation. Western governments dismissed the evidence. A letter from a U.S. lieutenant colonel at Los Alamos acknowledged the dangers of depleted uranium but warned they should not be publicized. In 1993, American cruise missiles destroyed the home of Iraqi artist Leila Attar, killing her and her husband in retaliation for an alleged assassination plot. No-fly zones were enforced with increasing aggression. Operation Desert Fox in 1998 dropped more bombs in four days than the entire 1991 air campaign. And at the Abu Dhabi arms fair, the merchants of death displayed their wares under fluorescent lights. The journalist tracked a Hellfire missile fragment from a destroyed ambulance in southern Lebanon to a factory hidden behind trees off an interstate highway in the American heartland. The manufacturer refused to comment. The Pentagon refused to investigate. The circle of violence and profit remained unbroken.

From a balcony overlooking the Mediterranean in Beirut, a journalist watches history repeat itself in layers. Crusader knights once massacred the city's population along the same coastal road where modern cruise missiles now streak toward their targets. This episode traces the fate of the Middle East's rulers and the fragile kingdoms they built. In Amman, King Hussein of Jordan, a man who survived countless assassination attempts and made peace with Israel, lies dying in a military hospital. Two Jordans come to bury their king: one of Palestinians and one of Bedouin, their grief genuine but their futures uncertain. The episode closes with the execution of Ottoman-era martyrs on that same square, a reminder that the struggle for independence and the cycles of repression have been turning in this region far longer than any living ruler can remember.

In the Shia neighborhoods of Baghdad, American missiles tore into crowded streets, killing dozens of civilians. When Baghdad fell on April 9th, the statue of Saddam came down, and behind it came an army of looters. Saddam was found in a hole in the ground near Tikrit, but his capture brought no peace. Abu Ghraib, sectarian militias, kidnap videos, and car bombs followed. The sack of Baghdad was complete, and the unraveling had only just begun.
