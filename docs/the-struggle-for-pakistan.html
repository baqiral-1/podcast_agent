The Struggle for Pakistan

A nation born in blood, partitioned from the subcontinent amid the largest mass migration in human history, struggles to define itself for seven decades. Military coups alternate with fragile democracies. Wars are fought and lost. An entire eastern wing breaks away. Generals drape martial law in religious garb while populist leaders promise revolution and deliver dynasties. Nuclear weapons are tested as children go hungry. And through it all, the question that haunted the founders remains unanswered: what is Pakistan for? This is the story of a country perpetually on the edge, where the next crisis is always one headline away.

Episode 1

Birth of a Nation: Partition, Identity, and State Formation

On a December afternoon in 2007, a woman stands through the sunroof of an armored vehicle, radiant and waving. Moments later, she is dead. But to understand that assassination, you must go back sixty years, to a partition that killed a million people and displaced fifteen million more. A brilliant lawyer transforms a scattered minority into a nation through sheer willpower and deliberate ambiguity. When the new state arrives on the map, it is not what anyone imagined. The founder dies within a year, and the fight over what his creation means has never stopped.

The story opens with the assassination of Benazir Bhutto in December 2007, then rewinds sixty years to the bloodiest birth of any modern nation. Mohammad Ali Jinnah, a secular lawyer in a Savile Row suit, forged the idea of Pakistan from the fears of a Muslim minority under colonial rule. His weapon was ambiguity: promising different things to different constituencies, invoking Islam without defining its role, demanding a homeland whose borders he left deliberately vague. The Lahore Resolution of 1940 declared India's ninety million Muslims a separate nation, but it took seven more years of political maneuvering, communal violence, and the catastrophic failure of the Cabinet Mission before the British finally agreed to partition. When Pakistan materialized on August 14, 1947, it was not the country its architects had envisioned. It was moth-eaten, divided into two wings separated by a thousand miles of hostile Indian territory. Religion was supposed to be the glue, but the new state's peoples spoke different languages, practiced different customs, and shared little beyond a nominal faith. Jinnah's first speech to the Constituent Assembly declared that religion would have nothing to do with the business of the state. Within months, Islamist leaders who had opposed Pakistan's creation were demanding it become a theocracy. The Objectives Resolution of 1949 tried to split the difference and satisfied no one. Meanwhile, the scramble for spoils began: evacuee properties looted, minorities harassed, the bureaucracy and military swelling as democratic institutions withered. Jinnah died thirteen months after independence. His successor, Liaquat Ali Khan, was assassinated in 1951. Within a decade, the army would seize power for the first time, beginning a pattern that would define Pakistan for generations.

Episode 2

Generals and Populists: Ayub, 1971, and Bhutto

While the country sleeps, democracy dies. A general seizes power and remakes the state in his own image, building a new capital and rigging elections through village councils he controls. But economic growth cannot paper over the chasm between east and west. When a cyclone kills hundreds of thousands and the government barely responds, the eastern wing votes for separation. What follows is military crackdown, genocide, Indian intervention, and the birth of Bangladesh. From the wreckage, a populist rises with promises of bread, clothing, and shelter. He delivers none of them.

On the night of October 7th, 1958, General Ayub Khan ended Pakistan's experiment with parliamentary democracy. The American ambassador called it the sort of thing Americans could understand. Ayub built a new capital at Islamabad, introduced a system of controlled local councils he called Basic Democracies, and won a rigged referendum with ninety-five percent of the vote. Economic growth followed, but it flowed overwhelmingly to West Pakistan while the eastern wing, home to the majority of the population, was treated as an internal colony. The economic gulf became a political chasm. When Cyclone Bhola killed hundreds of thousands in East Pakistan in November 1970, the government's callous response confirmed Bengali suspicions that they were expendable. In the elections that followed, Sheikh Mujib's Awami League won a landslide in the east, while Zulfikar Ali Bhutto's Pakistan People's Party swept the west. Neither man would compromise. When negotiations collapsed in March 1971, the army launched Operation Searchlight, a campaign of mass killing in East Pakistan that drove ten million refugees into India. India intervened. The Pakistani army surrendered. Bangladesh was born, and Pakistan lost more than half its population. From the ruins, Bhutto took power with promises of Islamic socialism: nationalized industries, land reform, bread and clothing and shelter for the poor. He crafted a new constitution that remains in force today. But Bhutto's populism was laced with authoritarianism. He crushed Baloch insurgents with military force, established a paramilitary intelligence agency, and bent every institution to his will. His concessions to Islamists, including declaring the Ahmadi community non-Muslim, planted seeds of sectarian violence that would bloom for decades. When rigged elections in 1977 triggered massive protests, the army moved again. General Zia ul-Haq arrested Bhutto at dawn, and the populist who had promised to remake Pakistan was eventually hanged.

Episode 3

Islamization and Cold War: Zia's Legacy and Fragile Democracy

A general seizes power at dawn and drapes martial law in Islamic garb. Sharia courts are established. Women's rights are gutted. A poet gives voice to democratic yearning from behind bars. Then the Soviets invade Afghanistan, and suddenly Pakistan's dictator becomes the West's indispensable ally. Billions in American dollars and Saudi-funded jihad flow through Pakistani intelligence. When Zia dies in a mysterious plane crash, democracy returns, but it is democracy in chains: two leaders rotate through power, each dismissed by presidents and generals who hold the real keys.

On the sultry night of July 4th, 1977, General Zia ul-Haq launched the coup that would transform Pakistan more profoundly than any event since partition. He promised elections in ninety days. Instead, he ruled for eleven years, systematically Islamizing the state: introducing Sharia benches, hudood ordinances that criminalized women for reporting rape, and blasphemy laws that would become instruments of persecution for decades. The poet Habib Jalib gave voice to resistance, and women's organizations fought back in the streets, but Zia's grip only tightened. Then came the Soviet invasion of Afghanistan, and everything changed. Pakistan became the frontline state in America's Cold War proxy battle. Billions of dollars flowed through the ISI to mujahideen fighters, while Saudi-funded madrassas along the border churned out a generation of jihadists. Zia played the Afghan card masterfully, extracting military and economic aid while pursuing a covert nuclear weapons program. When American diplomats objected, they were told to look the other way. The US Embassy in Islamabad was burned by a mob in 1979, and Zia went bicycling. The Ojhri Camp ammunition depot exploded in the heart of Rawalpindi in 1988, killing hundreds and destroying evidence of the arms pipeline. Months later, Zia himself was killed in a mysterious plane crash that has never been satisfactorily explained. Democracy returned, but it was democracy on a leash. Benazir Bhutto and Nawaz Sharif rotated through power through the 1990s, each government dismissed before completing its term. The military's corporate empire, built during Zia's years, made it the most powerful economic actor in the country. The Taliban emerged from the madrassas Zia had nurtured. Nuclear tests in 1998 made Pakistan a declared nuclear power but triggered economic sanctions. And the Kargil crisis of 1999 brought India and Pakistan to the brink of war, setting the stage for yet another military coup.

Episode 4

War on Terror, Endgames, and Pakistan's Unfinished Struggle

Another general, another coup. But this time, the planes that hit the Twin Towers thrust Pakistan's dictator onto the world stage with an ultimatum: you are with us or against us. What follows is a decade of double games: hunting terrorists with one hand while sheltering them with the other. Bin Laden is found a mile from Pakistan's military academy. A CIA agent shoots two men in Lahore. Drones strike tribal villages. And through it all, something remarkable happens: democracy, battered and flawed, refuses to die.

When General Pervez Musharraf seized power in October 1999, the pattern seemed depressingly familiar: another coup, another general promising reform. But September 11th, 2001, transformed Pakistan's fate. American Deputy Secretary of State Richard Armitage reportedly told Musharraf's intelligence chief: be prepared to be bombed back to the Stone Age. Pakistan pivoted overnight from Taliban ally to frontline partner in the War on Terror, and the contradictions proved impossible to manage. The ISI maintained links with Taliban factions it had cultivated for years. Pakistani territory became a sanctuary for al-Qaeda operatives. American drones struck targets in the tribal areas, killing militants and civilians alike, while Pakistani public opinion turned violently against Washington. Musharraf survived multiple assassination attempts, suspended the constitution, fired the Chief Justice, and triggered a lawyers' movement that shook his regime to its foundations. Benazir Bhutto returned from exile, negotiating a power-sharing deal with the general, only to be assassinated at a political rally in Rawalpindi in December 2007. The sympathy vote swept her husband Asif Ali Zardari into the presidency. The Pakistani Taliban launched a devastating campaign of bombings across the country. In 2009, the army finally moved against militants in Swat and South Waziristan. Governor Salmaan Taseer was murdered by his own bodyguard for defending a Christian woman accused of blasphemy. Then came the raid on Abbottabad: American special forces killed Osama bin Laden in a compound a mile from Pakistan's premier military academy, exposing either catastrophic intelligence failure or active complicity. And yet, through all of this, something unprecedented was happening. In 2013, Pakistan completed its first democratic transition from one elected government to another. The election was imperfect, shaped by dynastic politics and deep pockets, but it happened. Pakistan's unfinished struggle continues between military dominance and democratic aspiration, between extremism and a cultural renaissance happening beneath the surface.
