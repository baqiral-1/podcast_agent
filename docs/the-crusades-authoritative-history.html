The Crusades

Six episodes cover the preaching of holy war, the creation of crusader states, and the Muslim counteroffensive that reshaped the eastern Mediterranean. From the First Crusade to the loss of Outremer, strategy and faith collide across two centuries.

Episode 1

Origins and Triumph of the First Crusade

Urban II's call ignites a brutal journey that ends in Jerusalem.

The series opens in the fractured world of the eleventh-century West: pious fear, feuding lords, and a Church struggling to command the violence it had long condemned. Urban II's call at Clermont transforms penitential pilgrimage into holy war, and tens of thousands set out for Jerusalem. The episode follows the First Crusade's brutal march across Anatolia, the desperate siege of Antioch, and the cascade of visions and rumors that kept the army moving. When the crusaders finally reach Jerusalem, the siege and assault reveal both their endurance and their ruthlessness. The victory is staggering, but it is also precarious: a thin Latin foothold in a distant land. This is the crusades' origin story--faith fused to arms, and a triumph that contains the seeds of everything that follows.

Episode 2

Building Outremer and the Second Crusade

Settlement and compromise collide with the failed rescue of Edessa.

Conquest becomes settlement. The new crusader states build ports, castles, and courts while trying to govern a land where Franks are a small minority. Diplomacy and trade sit alongside raids and truces, and survival depends on alliances as much as swords. In Syria and Mesopotamia, Zangi's rise signals a Muslim revival, and the fall of Edessa in 1144 sends shockwaves across Europe. Bernard of Clairvaux helps ignite the Second Crusade, a vast expedition meant to restore the lost county. But the crusade collapses on the road and then at Damascus, where rival plans and local politics derail the campaign. The episode shows how Outremer is built through improvisation and compromise--and how Europe's first great rescue effort ends in humiliation, strengthening the very forces it aimed to defeat.

Episode 3

Nur al-Din, Egypt, and the Rise of Saladin

Muslim revival and Saladin's ascent tighten the ring around Outremer.

A new Muslim leadership redefines the struggle. Nur al-Din consolidates power in Syria, champions jihad, and turns Damascus into the pivot of a counter-crusade. In Egypt, a dynastic crisis draws in Nur al-Din's commanders, including Shirkuh and his young nephew Saladin. The episode follows the contest for Cairo, the fall of the Fatimids, and Saladin's rapid ascent from lieutenant to sultan. Once in control, Saladin unites Egypt and Syria, reorients the politics of the region, and begins to isolate the crusader states. Damascus and Aleppo become prizes, and truce gives way to ambition. The crusaders' survival now depends on an increasingly fragile balance between their internal rivalries and the tightening ring around them. It is the hinge of the series: from scattered conflict to a coordinated struggle for Jerusalem itself.

Episode 4

Hattin, Jerusalem Lost, and the Third Crusade Begins

Hattin's disaster shocks Europe and opens the Third Crusade.

In 1187 the balance breaks. Saladin draws the crusader army into the scorching trap of Hattin and destroys it, then retakes Jerusalem, shocking Latin Christendom. Europe responds with a Third Crusade led by emperors and kings, but even this grand coalition fractures. Frederick Barbarossa dies en route, and Richard the Lionheart and Philip of France arrive with competing agendas. The episode covers the brutal siege of Acre, Richard's victories at Arsuf and Jaffa, and the looming question of whether Jerusalem can actually be recovered. Europe raises money, vows, and new fleets as the news spreads. The Third Crusade begins with momentum and glory, but it is also a test of logistics, alliances, and political patience. The crusaders can win battles--but holding the Holy City proves far harder.

Episode 5

Richard, Saladin, and the Limits of Recovery

Jerusalem stays out of reach as diplomacy and rivalry take over.

Richard's campaign becomes a contest of endurance, diplomacy, and limited goals. He fortifies the coast, rebuilds Ascalon, and negotiates with Saladin even as his own coalition frays. Twice the crusaders approach Jerusalem, and twice they turn back, convinced they cannot hold it against a mobile field army and fragile supply lines. The episode follows the failed Jerusalem campaigns, the internal Frankish rivalries that undercut strategy, and the eventual truce that grants pilgrims access but leaves the city in Muslim hands. It is a story of limits: the limits of crusader manpower, of European unity, and of what battlefield brilliance can actually achieve. The Third Crusade ends not with recovery but with a negotiated stalemate that foreshadows the long decline to come.

Episode 6

Afterlives of Crusading: New Crusades, Mamluks, and the Fall of Outremer

Later crusades, Mamluk power, and the fall of Acre end the frontier.

After 1192, crusading changes shape. Popes reorganize the movement, new campaigns turn toward Egypt, and Frederick II briefly recovers Jerusalem through treaty rather than war. Yet the crusader states grow weaker as the Muslim world consolidates under the Mamluks. The episode traces the later expeditions of Louis IX, the rise of Baybars and the Mamluk military machine, and the looming Mongol threat that reshapes regional politics. Outremer survives through diplomacy and fortification, but the strategic tide has turned. In 1291 the last great stronghold, Acre, falls after a massive siege, ending two centuries of Latin rule in the Holy Land. Even as the frontier disappears, crusading rhetoric reshapes politics across Europe and the Mediterranean. The idea of crusade survives in memory, policy, and polemic long after Acre.
