Ghost Wars

A five-part account of how the anti-Soviet jihad became a permanent shadow war. It follows the CIA-ISI pipeline, the Taliban's rise, bin Laden's sanctuary, and the warnings that went unheeded before 9/11.

Episode 1

From Upheaval to Jihad: The War Is Built

Revolution, invasion, and a CIA pipeline set the rules of a war that will outgrow its architects.

An embassy riot in Islamabad hints at how quickly Afghanistan's revolution will spill across borders. The episode follows the Soviet misreading of Afghan politics, the escalation from covert aid to full invasion, and the rapid build out of a CIA pipeline that runs through Pakistan. Zia bargains for control, Saudi money follows, and intelligence gatekeepers decide which Afghan factions get paid and armed. We meet rising commanders and the hard line networks that prosper inside the flow of cash, rifles, and logistics. Ahmed Shah Massoud appears as a strategic counterweight, even as Washington embraces deniability. As Washington celebrates a proxy war that seems to be working, the war's internal logic hardens into a system that rewards the most uncompromising players. By the time outsiders recognize what they have built, it is already larger than any one agency can steer. The question is not whether the Soviets can be bled, but what will inherit their battlefield.

Episode 2

Victory Without Settlement: Civil War, Disengagement, and the Taliban's Rise

Victory dissolves into civil war as Pakistan and new jihad networks reshape Afghanistan's future.

The Soviets leave, but victory brings no settlement. The CIA pulls back as Kabul fractures, and the ISI shifts from partner to power broker, backing new factions and shaping who gets weapons, money, and legitimacy. Arab volunteers arrive in Peshawar, bin Laden's profile rises, and a jihad economy settles in. Washington debates whether to keep funding a war it has already "won," while Afghan commanders battle for Kabul and the battlefield moves to Jalalabad. Inside the U.S. mission, policy arguments harden; inside Pakistan, the proxy network deepens. Assassinations, coups, and Saudi double games widen the rift, and the Taliban's outline begins to appear in the chaos. Azzam's death opens space, and bin Laden starts testing independence. The episode follows the slide from withdrawal to neglect, showing how a victory narrative masked the birth of a new conflict. By the end, the question is no longer who defeated Moscow, but who will rule the ruins.

Episode 3

Sanctuary and Illusion: Taliban Rule, Bin Laden, and First U.S. Countermoves

Taliban rule hardens and bin Laden returns as Washington debates engagement or pursuit.

Khartoum, Manila, and a widening intelligence trail point to a network that is evolving faster than policy. The Taliban rise from Kandahar with Pakistani backing and Saudi money, and Washington flirts with engagement through a pipeline dream that promises stability. Hamid Karzai breaks with Kabul as Mullah Omar's movement gathers momentum. Inside Afghanistan, Kabul falls and a new regime offers order at a brutal price. Bin Laden leaves Sudan and finds sanctuary in Taliban territory, while U.S. counterterrorism units sharpen their focus but struggle for access. Missed manhunts, ambiguous warnings, and a hollowed out Afghan file leave Washington with a choice it cannot quite make. This episode tracks the collision between state building and jihadist sanctuary, and how the same actors can look like partners in one memo and targets in the next. By the time the Taliban rule hardens and bin Laden settles in, the warning lights are on, but the policy remains split.

Episode 4

At War in Fragments: Warnings, Missed Chances, and the Millennium

Embassy bombings bring retaliation, but missed chances and legal knots keep action at bay.

The embassy bombings push Washington into cruise missile retaliation, but the strike leaves bin Laden alive and the window closing. Inside the CIA and White House, warnings surge while legal and political ambiguity harden into paralysis. A commando offer, a hunting camp, and targeting rules collide with fragile intelligence. The Taliban become both interlocutors and obstacles, and Pakistan's cooperation proves conditional. As Tenet declares war without a war budget, the system generates more memos than missions. Cofer Black pushes outward while policymakers insist on tighter rules. The episode moves through the late 1990s: Hamburg and Kandahar, aviation plots, Kargil, the vanished JAWBREAKER proposal, and the Millennium panic. Each missed chance sharpens the sense of inevitability without producing decisive action. By the end, the United States has a clear enemy and no clear mechanism to reach him. The question is not whether a strike is justified, but whether it will arrive in time.

Episode 5

The Last Summer: Policy Drift to the Eve of 9/11

Predator debates, Massoud's warnings, and policy drift carry the story into summer 2001.

A new Pakistani liaison promises access, but every channel to Kandahar narrows. Massoud offers partnership, Washington hesitates, and a patchwork war takes shape with no settled policy. The Predator arrives as a proof of concept, then stalls as lawyers and operators debate rules for a strike. The USS Cole attack, the Tarnak farm, and the CIA's widening alarm push terrorism to the top of the agenda, yet decisions keep slipping. An interview request circles bin Laden's camp even as Washington still debates. Inside the bureaucracy, warnings multiply while Pakistan plays for leverage and bin Laden stays protected. As summer 2001 drains away, Massoud is isolated, the Taliban hold firm, and internal debates replace action. The episode traces how a decade of drift ends in a final warning the system cannot absorb. The last question before the catastrophe is painfully small: will anyone take the risk when the evidence is finally clear?
