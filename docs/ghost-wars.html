Ghost Wars

A seven-part account of how the CIA's secret war against the Soviets planted the seeds of September 11. Steve Coll's Ghost Wars traces the covert pipeline, the Taliban's rise, bin Laden's sanctuary, and the cascade of missed warnings and policy failures that left the most powerful intelligence agency on earth blind to an attack taking shape under its own watch.

Episode 1

1979: The Shocks That Opened the War

An embassy burns in Islamabad. Hostages are taken in Tehran. Soviet tanks cross into Afghanistan on Christmas Eve.

November 4, 1979. Rioters storm the U.S. embassy in Islamabad, hammering on the code-room vault while staff below try to electrocute them through the hatch. The wire shorts out in sparks. The pounding stops — not because of American defenses, but because the mob, convinced by rumor that Americans were behind the Grand Mosque siege in Mecca, eventually withdraws. The same day, Iranian students seize the embassy in Tehran. By Christmas Eve, Soviet tanks are crossing into Afghanistan. Three shocks in one year. This episode traces the decade of covert CIA involvement that precedes the invasion — the quiet build-up through the 1970s, the policy debate over whether to make Soviet intervention costly enough to deter, and the moment that debate becomes moot. Operation Cyclone begins: money, weapons, and Pakistani middlemen, flowing into a war whose architects believe they are winning a chess move against Moscow. The board, however, is much larger than they realize.

Episode 2

Escalating the Jihad, 1984–1988

Casey throws billions at the Afghan war. Bin Laden arrives. Nobody is paying close attention.

CIA Director William Casey sees Afghanistan as the chance to bleed Soviet communism on its own frontier, and he throws himself in with ideological fervor. Money scales from tens of millions to hundreds of millions. Weapons flow through Pakistan's ISI, which has its own agenda — backing Islamic factions that might give Islamabad influence over Kabul after the war, a hedge against India. Ahmad Shah Massoud fights with tactical brilliance in the Panjshir Valley. Arab volunteers arrive in Peshawar, drawn by the fusion of anti-communism and jihad. Among them, largely unremarked, is Osama bin Laden. Villages are bombed. Refugees flood into Pakistan by the millions. The war becomes a grinding stalemate — Soviets cannot win, mujahideen cannot expel them — but the CIA pipeline keeps the pressure on. Inside that pipeline, the most uncompromising commanders receive the most support. The architecture of a future conflict is being built, though no one yet sees it that way.

Episode 3

After Victory: Civil War and Strategic Drift

The Soviets leave. America leaves with them. Afghanistan is left to itself.

February 1989. The last Soviet troops cross the Oxus River into Tajikistan. The mujahideen have won. Washington declares victory and moves on — the Cold War is ending, the Gulf is burning, the budget is shrinking. Afghanistan drops off the map. In Kabul, the celebration lasts days. Then the fighting begins. Hekmatyar's forces shell the city. Massoud's Northern Alliance holds its ground. Saudi Arabia, Iran, and Pakistan each back their faction. The Afghan state collapses into warlord fiefdoms. Opium production explodes — drugs become currency. Civilians who survived the Soviet war now flee the civil war that victory produced. Bin Laden, watching from Peshawar, is disgusted by the spectacle — and by the American military presence in Saudi Arabia, home to the holiest cities in Islam. He has won a war. Now he is looking for another cause. The CIA, busy elsewhere, has stopped reading the file.

Episode 4

From Forgotten Afghanistan to Taliban Ascendancy

From Pakistani madrasas, a new movement emerges. Kabul falls in days.

The early 1990s. Kabul is a city at war with itself — neighborhoods shelled daily, no electricity, little food, thousands dead in factional fighting. Into this vacuum, from Pakistani madrasas, a new movement emerges: the Taliban. Mostly Pashtun, mostly educated across the border, they present themselves as an Islamic order that will end the warlords' chaos. Mullah Omar claims the title Commander of the Faithful. Pakistan's ISI, seeking Pashtun influence against India in post-war Afghanistan, provides quiet but critical support. The Taliban sweep through the south and east, often welcomed by populations exhausted by civil war. In September 1996, Kabul falls to them within days. Women are banned from schools and workplaces. Music stops. Non-Islamic artifacts are destroyed. Yet for populations that have lived under indiscriminate shelling, the order — however brutal — comes as relief. Washington, absorbed in Bosnia, the Oklahoma City bombing, and a shrinking defense budget, barely notices.

Episode 5

The Enemy in Afghanistan, 1996–1998

Bin Laden returns to Afghanistan. He has money, a network, and a declaration of war.

Osama bin Laden leaves Sudan and returns to Afghanistan as something far more dangerous than a foreign fighter: a wealthy man with a global network, a clear ideology, and a declaration of war against the United States. His relationship with Mullah Omar is pragmatic — money and fighters in exchange for sanctuary. In February 1996 he issues a manifesto. In August 1998, al-Qaeda strikes simultaneously in Nairobi and Dar es Salaam, killing more than 200. Clinton authorizes cruise missile strikes — one hits a pharmaceutical factory in Sudan that turns out to manufacture medicine, not nerve agents. The political cost of that error casts a shadow over every subsequent decision. Inside the CIA, analysts watching the growing network argue for more aggressive action. They are told the legal authorities aren't there. Meanwhile, bin Laden is building something new — and it is growing faster than anyone yet understands.

Episode 6

Failed Strikes and Policy Drift, 1998–2000

The CIA has a target. It loses the window. Then loses it again.

After the embassy bombings, the CIA knows where bin Laden is — or thinks it does. Kandahar. Tarnak Farms. A camp with a predictable pattern of movement. Plans are drawn up. Then cancelled. Then drawn up again. The legal authorities are contested. The risk of civilian casualties is deemed unacceptable. The window closes. Another window opens. It closes too. In Washington, inter-agency arguments harden into institutional positions, and institutional positions harden into inaction. The 9/11 Commission will later call this a 'failure of imagination.' This episode suggests something more specific: a failure of will — a bureaucracy that could identify a threat, track it, and still not find a way to act. Meanwhile, Pakistan continues sheltering Taliban leadership. Saudi Arabia continues funding mosques from Peshawar to Jakarta. And bin Laden, watching the American response to his attacks, draws a clear conclusion: they can be hit, and they will not hit back decisively.

Episode 7

The Last Warnings, 2000–September 10, 2001

The warnings are everywhere. The system is blinking red. Nobody connects the dots.

Summer 2001. CIA Director Tenet tells the 9/11 Commission the system was 'blinking red.' Warnings from field stations accumulate. Analysts flag suspicious patterns. A Phoenix field agent sends a memo noting that Middle Eastern men are training at American flight schools. It is filed. An FBI agent in Minneapolis, concerned about Zacarias Moussaoui's unusual interest in commercial aviation, requests a warrant to search his laptop. It is denied. At the National Security Council, counterterrorism czar Richard Clarke escalates his warnings to a level of alarm that colleagues find uncomfortable. He is told the principals will discuss it when they have time. On September 10, 2001, not a single senior official in the United States government believes an attack is twenty-four hours away. The episode closes on the night before — the preparations complete, the pilots sleeping, the intelligence system full of signal that no one has yet assembled into a picture.
