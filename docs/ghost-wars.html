Ghost Wars

For two decades, the CIA waged a secret war in Afghanistan, pouring billions into a covert pipeline that armed holy warriors, toppled a superpower, and then walked away. What filled the vacuum was far worse than anyone imagined. This series traces the hidden history of America's entanglement with Afghanistan, from the Soviet invasion through the rise of the Taliban and the hunt for Osama bin Laden, to the final desperate warnings that went unheeded before September 11th. It is a story of spies, warlords, missed chances, and catastrophic consequences.

Episode 1

The Soviet Invasion and the Birth of Covert War

The American embassy in Islamabad is under siege, staffers trapped in a smoke-filled vault as a mob storms the compound. Soviet armor pours into Afghanistan, and Zbigniew Brzezinski sees a chance to bleed Moscow. The CIA begins funneling weapons through Pakistan's intelligence service to Afghan guerrillas. A young Saudi named Osama bin Laden hauls supplies through border camps. And a Saudi intelligence chief dispatches his most trusted aide to manage the jihad. A covert war has begun that no one knows how to end.

In November 1979, a howling mob storms the American embassy in Islamabad, trapping staffers in a smoke-filled communications vault as they burn classified documents and fear for their lives. It is a harbinger of the chaos to come. Weeks later, Soviet tanks roll into Kabul, and the Cold War acquires its most dangerous new front. National Security Advisor Zbigniew Brzezinski immediately recognizes an opportunity: arm the Afghan resistance and turn Afghanistan into Moscow's Vietnam. President Carter authorizes a covert action program, and the CIA begins building a weapons pipeline that will eventually become the largest covert operation in American history. Howard Hart, a veteran case officer, arrives in Peshawar to manage the program on the ground, forging a critical and deeply complicated partnership with Pakistan's Inter-Services Intelligence directorate. The Pakistanis insist on controlling which Afghan factions receive weapons, a decision with consequences that will echo for decades. Meanwhile, a wealthy young Saudi named Osama bin Laden travels to the Pakistani border, hauling construction equipment and supplies to Afghan camps. Saudi intelligence chief Prince Turki al-Faisal dispatches his trusted aide Ahmed Badeeb to coordinate the kingdom's role in the jihad. The covert war is up and running, but its architects have no exit strategy and no idea what forces they are helping to unleash.

Episode 2

Stingers, Casey, and the Soviet Withdrawal

CIA Director William Casey pushes to escalate the covert war, personally visiting the region and demanding bolder action. In the mountains of the Panjshir Valley, Ahmad Shah Massoud emerges as the most gifted guerrilla commander of the conflict. A wave of international terrorism prompts new authorities for aggressive operations. In Peshawar, Osama bin Laden pours money into camps and attracts Arab volunteers. As Soviet withdrawal looms, a CIA officer arrives in Islamabad to manage the war's volatile endgame. Victory is near, but what comes after?

CIA Director William Casey, a gruff veteran of wartime intelligence, throws himself into escalating America's secret war in Afghanistan. He visits the region personally, pushes for the introduction of Stinger anti-aircraft missiles that will transform the battlefield, and champions aggressive new counterterrorism authorities in response to a wave of international attacks. In the rugged mountains of northeastern Afghanistan, Ahmad Shah Massoud wages a sophisticated guerrilla campaign against Soviet forces in the Panjshir Valley, earning a reputation as the most capable and politically astute of all the mujahedin commanders. His independence and military brilliance will make him both indispensable and deeply threatening to Pakistan's intelligence service, which backs rival Pashtun factions. In the border city of Peshawar, Osama bin Laden accelerates his transformation from wealthy donor to operational leader, building camps, attracting Arab volunteers from across the Muslim world, and forging relationships that will form the nucleus of a future network. The Soviets, bleeding from a war they cannot win, begin signaling their intention to withdraw. Milton Bearden, a swaggering CIA officer, arrives in Islamabad to manage the endgame. But as the prospect of victory approaches, no one in Washington is seriously planning for what happens when the Soviets leave and the guns fall silent.

Episode 3

Civil War, Policy Drift, and New Threats

The last Soviet soldier crosses the Friendship Bridge, and America immediately loses interest. Diplomat Ed McWilliams raises the alarm about Pakistan funneling weapons to extremists, and is punished for his honesty. The CIA and State Department pursue competing agendas as Afghanistan disintegrates into civil war. Covert action authority quietly expires. Then a truck bomb detonates beneath the World Trade Center, and suddenly the threat has come home. But all aid to Afghanistan has been cut, leaving a power vacuum that the worst actors rush to fill.

In February 1989, the last Soviet soldiers withdraw across the Friendship Bridge into Uzbekistan, and the United States promptly begins to lose interest in Afghanistan. The covert war that consumed billions of dollars is declared a victory, but no one has planned for the peace. Diplomat Ed McWilliams, stationed in Islamabad, raises urgent warnings that Pakistan's intelligence service is funneling American-supplied weapons to the most extreme Islamist factions while sidelining moderate commanders. His reward is professional retaliation. Peter Tomsen is appointed special envoy and attempts to forge a coherent American policy, but finds himself trapped between a CIA that resists oversight and a State Department with shrinking leverage. By 1992, the authority for covert action in Afghanistan expires, and American engagement effectively ends. The country descends into a savage civil war as rival mujahedin factions turn their weapons on each other, reducing Kabul to rubble. Then, on February 26, 1993, a truck bomb detonates in the parking garage beneath the World Trade Center in New York, and the CIA's Counterterrorist Center is thrust into urgent new investigations that trace back to networks forged during the Afghan jihad. The connection between the covert war and the emerging terrorist threat is becoming impossible to ignore, yet all bilateral aid to Afghanistan has been cut, leaving the country to fracture into fiefdoms ruled by warlords and fanatics.

Episode 4

Taliban Ascendant: Kandahar to Kabul

From the chaos of southern Afghanistan, a mysterious one-eyed cleric named Mullah Omar unites Pashtun fighters under a new banner: the Taliban. Fueled by a covert pipeline of Pakistani support, they sweep from Kandahar toward Kabul with terrifying speed. American energy executives at Unocal chase a pipeline deal while diplomats see a chance to stabilize the country. When the Taliban seize the capital, a CIA station chief flies secretly into the city. In the northern mountains, Ahmad Shah Massoud withdraws his battered forces, preparing for a last stand.

In the mid-1990s, a new force erupts from the anarchy of southern Afghanistan. Mullah Mohammad Omar, a reclusive one-eyed village cleric, gathers a band of former religious students and launches a campaign to impose order through harsh Islamic law. The Taliban movement sweeps northward from Kandahar with stunning speed, fueled by a covert pipeline of weapons, money, and recruits flowing from Pakistan's intelligence service. American officials struggle to understand who these new players are and what they want. Energy executives at the Unocal corporation pursue an ambitious plan for a trans-Afghan gas pipeline that requires Taliban cooperation, and the Clinton administration cautiously explores the group as a potential stabilizing force. It is a catastrophic misreading. When the Taliban capture Kabul in September 1996, they publicly execute the former communist president and impose a regime of extraordinary brutality, banning music, education for girls, and virtually all forms of public life. CIA station chief Gary Schroen flies secretly into the fallen capital to assess the situation. In the northern mountains, Ahmad Shah Massoud withdraws his battered Northern Alliance forces and begins regrouping for a prolonged resistance. Meanwhile, Osama bin Laden, expelled from Sudan, arrives in Afghanistan and finds in Taliban-ruled territory the perfect sanctuary from which to plan his most ambitious operations yet.

Episode 5

The Hunt for Bin Laden

George Tenet takes command of the CIA and throws himself into the hunt. A defector named Jamal al-Fadl walks into American hands, revealing the inner workings of bin Laden's network for the first time. Plans to capture bin Laden take shape, then collapse amid agonizing debates over lethal authority. Twin embassy bombings kill hundreds, and seventy-five cruise missiles are launched in retaliation, but bin Laden survives. The CIA deepens its alliance with Massoud, yet the political will to act decisively remains agonizingly out of reach.

George Tenet is sworn in as CIA Director and attacks his new role with restless energy, but the challenge of Osama bin Laden proves maddeningly elusive. A breakthrough arrives when Jamal al-Fadl, a disillusioned member of al-Qaeda, defects and walks into American hands, providing the first detailed map of bin Laden's organizational structure, finances, and ambitions. The intelligence is extraordinary, but translating it into action proves far harder. The first formal plan to capture bin Laden at his compound near Kandahar takes shape, involving Afghan tribal agents, but the operation is repeatedly debated, delayed, and ultimately shelved amid paralyzing disagreements over risk, legality, and lethal authority. Then, on August 7, 1998, massive truck bombs detonate simultaneously outside the American embassies in Nairobi and Dar es Salaam, killing over two hundred people and wounding thousands. President Clinton orders retaliatory strikes, and seventy-five Tomahawk cruise missiles slam into targets in Afghanistan and Sudan. Bin Laden survives, and the strikes are widely criticized as ineffective. The CIA builds a new partnership with Uzbek intelligence and deepens its uneasy alliance with Ahmad Shah Massoud, funneling money and equipment to the Northern Alliance. Millennium plots bring a cascade of alarming near-misses as the year 2000 approaches. Yet despite mounting evidence that al-Qaeda is planning something catastrophic, the bureaucratic and political obstacles to decisive action remain insurmountable.

Episode 6

Countdown to September 11

A bold new idea emerges: arm the Predator drone with missiles and use it to hunt bin Laden from the sky. But bureaucratic warfare between the CIA and Pentagon stalls the program for critical months. In Pakistan, General Musharraf consolidates power after a coup, complicating every American calculation. The CIA's alliance with Massoud deepens but cannot deliver the decisive blow. Warning lights flash red across the intelligence community through the summer of 2001. Then, on September 9th, two assassins posing as journalists detonate a bomb that changes everything.

The final act unfolds with agonizing slowness. A revolutionary idea takes shape within the CIA: mount Hellfire missiles on the Predator surveillance drone and use it to strike bin Laden directly. The technology works in tests, but a bureaucratic war between the CIA and the Pentagon over who controls the weapon and who bears responsibility for its use delays deployment for months that will prove catastrophic. In Pakistan, General Pervez Musharraf seizes power in a military coup, adding another layer of complexity to America's already tangled relationship with Islamabad. The CIA deepens its partnership with Ahmad Shah Massoud, sending teams into the Panjshir Valley to coordinate intelligence and plan operations against al-Qaeda and the Taliban. But Massoud grows frustrated by Washington's inability to commit to decisive action. Through the spring and summer of 2001, warning lights across the American intelligence community flash at unprecedented levels. Threat reporting reaches a crescendo, with CIA analysts warning that a major attack is imminent. George Tenet later describes his hair as being on fire. Yet the system fails to connect the dots, and the policy machinery grinds to a halt amid transitions and turf battles. On September 9, 2001, two men posing as journalists detonate a bomb hidden in a video camera during an interview with Ahmad Shah Massoud, mortally wounding America's most important Afghan ally just forty-eight hours before the world changes forever.
