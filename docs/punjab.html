Punjab: A History from Aurangzeb to Mountbatten

Five rivers carved a land that empires could never hold for long. From the Sufi shrines of the Delhi Sultanate to Mughal Lahore's golden age, from the bloody forging of the Sikh Khalsa to Ranjit Singh's dazzling kingdom, Punjab's story is one of relentless reinvention under fire. Invasions shatter it, colonial rule reshapes it, and the nightmare of Partition tears it apart. Through it all, the people of the five rivers fight, adapt, and endure. What price did they pay for a freedom that arrived drenched in blood?

Episode 1

Ancient Roots to Mughal Collapse

Beneath the splendor of Mughal Lahore, a storm is gathering. Sufi saints hold together a fractured society while emperors build monuments to eternity. But when Guru Gobind Singh's fortress falls and Aurangzeb dies, the entire order unravels. Banda Singh Bahadur's uprising sends shockwaves through the province. Governor Zakariya Khan unleashes savage reprisals against the Sikhs, yet nothing can stop the coming avalanche. Then Ahmad Shah Abdali crosses the frontier, and Punjab plunges into chaos. Who will rise from the wreckage?

The story of Punjab begins not with borders but with rivers, saints, and conquerors. For centuries, Sufi mystics wove together the spiritual fabric of a land perpetually caught between invading armies. Under the Mughals, Lahore became one of the world's great cities, rivaling Constantinople and Isfahan in grandeur. But the empire's magnificent facade concealed fatal cracks. When Guru Gobind Singh was besieged at Anandpur in 1704 and forced into a devastating retreat, the Sikh community was transformed forever. Aurangzeb's death three years later removed the last force holding the Mughal machine together. Into the vacuum surged Banda Singh Bahadur, whose rebellion set the province ablaze and terrified the imperial court. Governor Zakariya Khan responded with bounties on Sikh heads and mass executions, but resistance only hardened. The real blow came from beyond the Khyber Pass: Ahmad Shah Abdali invaded in 1748, launching a cycle of Afghan raids that would devastate Punjab for decades. By the mid-eighteenth century, the old order lay shattered. But amid the ruins, new powers were stirring, and the cunning Adina Beg Khan was already playing all sides against each other.

Episode 2

Afghan Dominance to Ranjit Singh's Empire

Punjab is a chessboard, and everyone wants to be king. Adina Beg Khan plays Afghans against Marathas against Sikhs with dazzling cunning, but no schemer lives forever. From the wreckage of failed empires, Sikh warrior confederacies carve out territory with sword and cannon. Then a smallpox-scarred teenager named Ranjit Singh begins unifying them all. He seizes Lahore, claims the Kohinoor diamond, and conquers Kashmir. His kingdom becomes the most powerful in the subcontinent. But the moment he dies, the knives come out, and two catastrophic wars with the British seal Punjab's fate.

In the late eighteenth century, Punjab is a killing ground contested by Afghan invaders, Maratha armies, and rising Sikh confederacies. The mastermind Adina Beg Khan navigates this deadly landscape with breathtaking duplicity, switching allegiances to survive. But survival is not mastery, and after the Afghans are finally driven out, the Sikh misls carve the province among themselves in a patchwork of competing warlords. From this chaos emerges one of history's most remarkable figures: Ranjit Singh, a young leader scarred by smallpox and forged by war. Through a combination of military genius, shrewd diplomacy, and sheer force of personality, he welds the warring factions into a unified Sikh kingdom that stretches from the Sutlej to the Khyber Pass. He secures the legendary Kohinoor diamond, conquers Kashmir, and builds an army trained by European mercenaries that rivals anything on the subcontinent. For forty years, his court at Lahore dazzles visitors and defies the expanding British Empire. Yet the kingdom depends entirely on one man's iron will. When Ranjit Singh dies in 1839, the Lahore Durbar descends into a spiral of assassinations and succession crises. Two devastating Anglo-Sikh wars follow, and the most powerful indigenous state in India collapses in barely a decade.

Episode 3

Annexation, 1857, and Imperial High Noon

Lord Dalhousie swallows Punjab whole in 1849, and a new breed of colonial officer arrives to remake the province. John Nicholson rules Rawalpindi with terrifying intensity, worshipped by some, feared by all. When mutiny erupts across India in May 1857, John Lawrence races to disarm suspect regiments before rebellion spreads. Nicholson force-marches through punishing heat to storm Delhi, turning Punjab into the launchpad for British reconquest. In the decades that follow, canal colonies transform the landscape and the province becomes the empire's sword arm. But at what cost?

The British annexation of Punjab in 1849 was swift, clinical, and transformative. Lord Dalhousie dismantled the Sikh kingdom with bureaucratic efficiency, and a new generation of imperial officers took charge of a province they barely understood. Among them was John Nicholson, a man of volcanic temper and legendary reputation who governed Rawalpindi with an authority that bordered on the messianic. When the Great Uprising of 1857 erupted in Meerut and spread across northern India, Punjab became the critical battleground. John Lawrence, the province's chief commissioner, moved with ruthless speed to disarm suspect regiments in Lahore before rebellion could take hold. Nicholson led an exhausting forced march to suppress mutineers at Gurdaspur, then drove his column toward Delhi. His assault on the Mughal capital proved decisive in turning the tide of the entire rebellion. In the aftermath, Punjab was rewarded with investment and transformed by canal colonies that reshaped its agricultural landscape. The province became the British Empire's most vital military recruiting ground, sending Sikh and Muslim soldiers to fight across the globe. The Muslim League was founded, new political identities crystallized, and beneath the surface of imperial stability, the forces that would eventually tear Punjab apart were quietly gathering strength.

Episode 4

Freedom, Partition, and Aftermath

Gandhi's call for satyagraha electrifies a restless Punjab, forging an unlikely alliance between Congress and the Muslim League. For one shining moment, Hindus, Muslims, and Sikhs march together against the empire. But the alliance fractures, and deeper fault lines crack open with terrifying speed. As independence approaches, the question is no longer whether the British will leave, but whether Punjab can survive their departure. The answer comes in blood, fire, and the largest mass migration in human history. No one who lived through it was left unchanged.

The aftermath of the First World War brought a new kind of politics to Punjab. Gandhi's satyagraha movement swept through the province, creating extraordinary scenes of Hindu-Muslim-Sikh unity against British rule. The Jallianwala Bagh massacre of 1919 became a defining atrocity that radicalized a generation. For a time, the Khilafat and Non-Cooperation movements united communities that had coexisted for centuries. But the alliance was fragile, built on shared opposition rather than shared vision. As constitutional negotiations dragged on through the 1930s and 1940s, communal identities hardened. The Muslim League's demand for Pakistan gained unstoppable momentum, and Punjab found itself at the epicenter of the coming storm. The province that had been the empire's most loyal recruit was now its most dangerous flashpoint. When Partition was announced in August 1947, Punjab did not merely divide; it exploded. Communal violence on a scale that stunned even hardened observers engulfed both sides of the new border. Trains arrived at stations filled with corpses. Entire villages were emptied overnight. Millions fled in both directions in the largest mass migration in recorded history, carrying whatever they could and leaving behind everything they had known. The five rivers that gave Punjab its name now ran through two separate nations, and the wounds of that division would shape the subcontinent for generations to come.
